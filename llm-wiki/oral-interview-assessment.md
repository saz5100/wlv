# Oral Interview Assessment

The University of Worcester English Equivalency test uses an oral interview model for Section A (Reading). Questions 3–6 are delivered orally during a Collaborate interview, meaning candidates must listen carefully and respond verbally without seeing the questions in advance.

## How It Works

1. **Before the interview**: Candidates receive the source extract and questions 1–2 (multiple-choice retrieval and inference).
2. **At the interview start**: Questions 3–6 are emailed to the candidate and discussed orally.
3. **During the interview**: The interviewer asks each question verbally; the candidate responds orally.
4. **Assessment**: Responses are assessed on comprehension, interpretation, and inference.

## Skills Tested

- **Listening comprehension**: Understanding the question as spoken
- **Verbal articulation**: Expressing ideas clearly and coherently
- **Quick thinking**: Formulating responses without written preparation time
- **Evidence selection**: Identifying and referencing textual evidence verbally
- **Paraphrasing**: Restating ideas in your own words

## Preparation Strategies

### 1. Practise Verbal Responses
- Read a short extract and ask yourself comprehension questions
- Practise answering aloud, timing your responses
- Record yourself and evaluate clarity and coherence

### 2. Develop Note-Taking Skills
- Annotate the extract before the interview
- Highlight key quotations and themes
- Prepare brief notes on character relationships and writer's views

### 3. Practise Common Question Types
- **Character interpretation** (Q4): 'What does this description reveal about X?'
- **Relationship summary** (Q5): 'How would you describe the relationship between X and Y?'
- **View summary** (Q6): 'Summarise X's views on Y in your own words.'

### 4. Build Confidence
- Simulate interview conditions with a study partner
- Practise maintaining eye contact with the camera
- Use fillers sparingly ('Let me think about that...')

## Common Pitfalls

- **Rambling**: Keep responses focused and structured
- **Forgetting evidence**: Always support points with textual references
- **Reading from notes**: Maintain natural speech patterns
- **Over-explaining**: Be concise — aim for 2–3 well-supported points per question
