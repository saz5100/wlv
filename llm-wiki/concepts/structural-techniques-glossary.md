# Structural Techniques Glossary — AQA Paper 1

A comprehensive reference of structural techniques tested in AQA Paper 1 Q3. Structure is the **architecture** of the text — how the writer organises and sequences content to control the reader's experience. Unlike language (Q2), structure looks at the **bigger picture**: where the writer puts things, when, and why.

---

## Opening Techniques

### In Medias Res
- **Definition:** Starting the text in the middle of the action
- **Example:** "The gunshot echoed through the valley before anyone could scream." (No scene-setting, straight into the moment)
- **Effect:** Grabs attention immediately; creates urgency and intrigue; forces the reader to piece together context

### Establishing Shot (Wide Opening)
- **Definition:** Beginning with a broad description of the setting before zooming in
- **Example:** "The village stretched along the valley floor, a scatter of grey stone cottages huddled against the hillside. In the largest of these, a candle flickered."
- **Effect:** Orients the reader in time and place; creates a calm or neutral starting point before tension builds

### Character-First Opening
- **Definition:** Introducing a character immediately before any setting
- **Example:** "He was old before his time, his face a map of hardship and disappointment."
- **Effect:** Creates immediate empathy or curiosity about the character; makes the reader invested

### Philosophical or Reflective Opening
- **Definition:** Beginning with a general statement or reflection
- **Example:** "There are moments in life that change everything, though you never recognise them when they happen."
- **Effect:** Establishes theme and tone; invites the reader into a reflective mode

## Focus and Perspective

### Zoom In
- **Definition:** Moving from a wide view to a close-up detail
- **Example:** "The beach stretched for miles, empty and windswept. **But it was the single footprint in the sand that caught his attention.** "
- **Effect:** Signals importance; tells the reader to pay attention to the specific detail being zoomed on

### Zoom Out
- **Definition:** Pulling back from a close detail to a wider perspective
- **Example:** "She stared at the photograph in her hands, tears streaming down her face. **Beyond the window, the city continued its indifferent hum.** "
- **Effect:** Provides context or contrast; can create a sense of insignificance or isolation

### Focus Shift
- **Definition:** Moving attention from one subject to another
- **Example:** "While the children played in the garden, their mother watched from the kitchen window, her hands trembling as she held the letter."
- **Effect:** Creates contrast or irony; can reveal hidden emotions or parallel narratives

### Perspective Shift
- **Definition:** Changing the narrative viewpoint (e.g. from outside to inside a character's mind)
- **Example:** "To the onlooker, he appeared calm. **But inside, his thoughts were a storm.** "
- **Effect:** Reveals the gap between appearance and reality; creates dramatic irony

### Third-Person Limited Narration
- **Definition:** The narrator follows one character's thoughts and feelings
- **Example:** The narrative stays inside the protagonist's head, describing only what they see, hear, and feel
- **Effect:** Creates closeness and empathy; limits information to build suspense
- *See dedicated page: [[third-person-limited-narration]]*

## Time and Sequence

### Chronological Order
- **Definition:** Events presented in the order they happen
- **Effect:** Clear and easy to follow; can build tension step by step

### Flashback / Analepsis
- **Definition:** A shift to an earlier time
- **Example:** "Years later, he would remember that morning. **The sun had been shining then, and she had been laughing.** "
- **Effect:** Provides background or context; creates contrast between past and present; evokes nostalgia or regret

### Flash-Forward / Prolepsis
- **Definition:** A glimpse of a future event
- **Example:** "He didn't know it then, but **this would be the last time he saw the sea.** "
- **Effect:** Creates dramatic irony; builds anticipation and dread

### Time Shift / Temporal Jump
- **Definition:** Skipping forward in time without a full flashback structure
- **Example:** "**Days passed. Then weeks.** The snow melted, and the first green shoots appeared."
- **Effect:** Speeds up the narrative; suggests monotony or the passage of significant time

### Ellipsis (Temporal)
- **Definition:** A gap in time where events are deliberately omitted
- **Example:** A scene ends with a character in danger; the next scene begins a week later
- **Effect:** Creates mystery; allows the reader to imagine what happened in the gap

### Slow Time / Time Expansion
- **Definition:** Describing a brief moment in great detail
- **Example:** "The bullet left the chamber. **In that split second, he saw his life flash before him — every mistake, every joy, every goodbye.** "
- **Effect:** Heightens tension; emphasises the significance of the moment

### Accelerated Time
- **Definition:** Covering a long period in a few sentences
- **Example:** "**The years blurred into one another** — school, university, marriage, children, the slow greying of his hair."
- **Effect:** Suggests monotony, inevitability, or the passage of time without significant change

## Pacing and Rhythm

### Short Sentences / Fragments
- **Definition:** Brief, punchy sentences that quicken pace
- **Example:** "He ran. Faster. His lungs burned. The footsteps behind him grew louder."
- **Effect:** Creates urgency, panic, or tension; mirrors a racing heart or quickening breath

### Long, Flowing Sentences
- **Definition:** Complex, multi-clause sentences that slow the pace
- **Example:** "The mist hung low over the valley, curling around the ancient trees like ghostly fingers, muffling every sound, softening every edge, until the world seemed to exist in a hushed, grey dream."
- **Effect:** Creates atmosphere, calm, or reflection; slows the reader down

### Pace Variation
- **Definition:** Alternating between fast and slow pacing
- **Example:** A long, atmospheric description (slow) followed by a short, urgent sentence (fast)
- **Effect:** Controls the reader's emotional journey; creates peaks and troughs of tension

### Paragraph Length Variation
- **Definition:** Alternating between long and short paragraphs
- **Example:** A dense, detailed paragraph establishing the scene, followed by a single-sentence paragraph: "**Then everything changed.** "
- **Effect:** Short paragraphs signal importance or a turning point; long paragraphs create immersion

## Structural Patterns

### Cyclical Structure
- **Definition:** The text ends by returning to an image, phrase, or idea from the beginning
- **Example:** Opens with "The sea was calm" — closes with "The sea was calm once more, as if nothing had happened"
- **Effect:** Creates a sense of completion or inevitability; emphasises that the character has or has not changed

### Linear Progression
- **Definition:** Events unfold in a clear, forward-moving sequence from start to finish
- **Effect:** Straightforward and easy to follow; builds tension steadily

### Non-Linear Structure
- **Definition:** Events presented out of chronological order
- **Effect:** Creates mystery; reveals information deliberately; mimics memory or trauma

### Juxtaposition (Structural)
- **Definition:** Placing two contrasting scenes or ideas next to each other
- **Example:** A peaceful domestic scene immediately followed by a violent confrontation
- **Effect:** Highlights the contrast; makes each scene more powerful by proximity

### Contrasting Sections
- **Definition:** Dividing the text into clearly different parts (e.g. before/after an event)
- **Example:** "Before the accident" section vs "After the accident" section
- **Effect:** Makes the change or impact visible; creates a clear structural turning point

### Cause and Effect Chain
- **Definition:** One event leads logically to the next
- **Effect:** Creates a sense of inevitability; builds narrative momentum

## Progression and Movement

### Movement from Outside to Inside
- **Definition:** Starting with external description and moving to internal thoughts
- **Example:** Describes the setting → describes the character's appearance → reveals the character's inner fear
- **Effect:** Layers information; builds intimacy with the character gradually

### Movement from General to Specific
- **Definition:** Broad observation narrows to a focused detail
- **Example:** "The city was vast and indifferent. But one window — just one — glowed with a warm, yellow light."
- **Effect:** Creates emphasis; guides the reader's attention to what matters

### Rising Action / Tension Curve
- **Definition:** Tension builds gradually through the text to a peak
- **Effect:** Creates emotional engagement; the peak (climax) is more satisfying because of the build-up

### Climax
- **Definition:** The peak of tension or the turning point of the narrative
- **Effect:** The most intense moment; often where conflict is resolved or comes to a head

### Anti-Climax / Bathos
- **Definition:** A sudden drop from a tense moment to something mundane or disappointing
- **Effect:** Creates humour, relief, or a deliberate let-down; can subvert expectations
- *See dedicated page: [[anticlimax]]*

### Resolution / Denouement
- **Definition:** The aftermath of the climax where loose ends are tied up
- **Effect:** Provides closure; allows the reader to process the events

## Linking and Cohesion

### Discourse Markers
- **Definition:** Words or phrases that signal the relationship between ideas
- **Example:** "**However**, **meanwhile**, **in contrast**, **as a result**, **finally**"
- **Effect:** Guides the reader through the argument or narrative; creates clear structure

### Repetition (Structural)
- **Definition:** Repeating a word, phrase, or image at key moments
- **Example:** The phrase "**the wall**" appears at the beginning, middle, and end, each time with a different meaning
- **Effect:** Creates a motif or leitmotif; reinforces theme; shows change over time

### Leitmotif / Motif
- **Definition:** A recurring image, phrase, or idea throughout the text
- **Example:** "Rain" appears every time the character is sad; "Birds" appear every time the character feels free
- **Effect:** Creates thematic unity; reinforces the emotional or symbolic meaning

### Parallelism
- **Definition:** Structuring different parts of the text in a similar way
- **Example:** Two characters facing the same situation described in matching sentence structures
- **Effect:** Invites comparison; emphasises similarity or difference

---

## Dialogue and Interaction

### Dialogue Placement
- **Definition:** Where the writer chooses to include or withhold speech
- **Effect:** Dialogue can break up description, reveal character, or accelerate pace; withholding dialogue at a key moment can increase tension

### Internal Monologue
- **Definition:** A character's inner thoughts presented directly
- **Effect:** Provides intimate access to a character's mental state; builds empathy

### Contrasting Speech Patterns
- **Definition:** Different characters use different registers or dialects
- **Effect:** Reveals social class, education, or personality differences without stating them directly

## How to Use This Glossary for Q3

1. **Read the whole extract first** — Q3 asks about the whole text, not one section
2. **Identify structural shifts** — Where does the focus change? Where does the pace change? Where does time jump?
3. **Track the beginning, middle, and end** — What does the writer establish in each section?
4. **Ask WHY** — Don't just say "the writer shifts focus". Say WHY: "The writer shifts focus from the peaceful setting to the character's inner turmoil to **juxtapose the calm environment with the character's emotional distress**"
5. **Use structural vocabulary** — "at the beginning", "the focus then shifts", "the pace quickens when", "the cyclical return to..."

> **Model paragraph:** "At the start of the extract, the writer establishes a calm, reflective mood through long, flowing sentences and a wide establishing shot of the landscape. **The focus then shifts** dramatically when the narrator spots the figure in the water. The paragraphs become shorter, the sentences more fragmented, and the pace accelerates. This structural shift **creates a jolt for the reader**, mirroring the character's sudden transition from peace to panic."
