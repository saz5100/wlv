# Indicative Standard in AQA Mark Schemes

An **Indicative Standard** refers to the specific examples of student responses or analytical points that examiners use to determine which "Level of Response" a piece of work falls into. 

While Level Descriptors provide the general quality of the writing (e.g., "perceptive" or "clear"), the indicative standards provide the actual content or "points" that would justify that level for a specific question.

## Purpose of Indicative Standards

- **Illustrate expectations:** Show examiners what a response at a given level looks like.
- **Guide marking:** Provide concrete examples to support level determination.
- **Ensure consistency:** Help all examiners apply the same standards across different scripts.

## Application and Examples

Indicative standards vary by question type and paper. Examples from the AQA 8700/1 Sample Mark Scheme (June 2026) and the November 2024 Paper include:

### Q2 (Language Analysis)
High-level indicative standards demonstrate perceptive analysis of word choices, connotations, and sentence forms.
- **Sample MS (June 2026):** Analysis of the word 'ugly' and the phrase 'beyond redemption' to explore the writer's language choices in depth.
- **Nov 2024 Paper:** The ability to explain the *effect* of a specific simile, such as how "like a snapped spine" conveys a sense of instability.

### Q3 (Structural Analysis)
Level 4 indicative standards demonstrate structural analysis of elements such as:
- Foreshadowing
- Pivotal points
- First-person perspective
- How these elements are used to create tension.

### Q4 (Evaluation)
Level 4 indicative standards demonstrate perceptive evaluation. For example, evaluating a hyena's behaviour as 'more amusing' than threatening through the analysis of:
- Repetition
- Onomatopoeia
- Anti-climax

## How to Use Indicative Standards

- **Reference point:** Use the indicative standard as a benchmark for the level.
- **Not exhaustive:** The indicative standard is not a complete list of all possible valid responses.
- **Guide, not rule:** Examiners should use professional judgement alongside the indicative standard.
