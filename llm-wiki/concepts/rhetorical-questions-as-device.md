# Rhetorical Questions as a Device

## Definition

A **rhetorical question** is a question asked for effect, not for an answer. The answer is either obvious, implied, or left for the reader to contemplate. The writer does not expect — and usually does not want — a direct response.

Commonly known as *erotema* (Greek: "to question").

## Core Distinction

| Type | Expects Answer? | Example |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Literal question | Yes | "What time does the train arrive?" |
| Rhetorical question | No | "Who could possibly ignore such suffering?" |

## Functions in Writing (Q5)

Rhetorical questions serve several strategic purposes in creative and persuasive writing:

### 1. Engage the Reader
They draw the reader in by making them mentally answer the question themselves.

> *"How could anyone forget that night?"*

The reader subconsciously thinks: *I couldn't. It was unforgettable.*

### 2. Create Emphasis
A rhetorical question adds weight to a point the writer is about to make or has just made.

> *"Was this justice? Of course it wasn't."*

The question sets up the emphatic answer.

### 3. Introduce a Topic Change (Transition)
Questions can signal a shift in focus, replacing explicit transition phrases.

> *"But what about those left behind?"*

This pivots the reader's attention to a new angle without needing "however" or "meanwhile".

### 4. Build Persuasive Force
In argumentative or persuasive writing, rhetorical questions challenge the reader's assumptions.

> *"Do we really need another millionaire when children go hungry?"*

The question carries its own judgement — the reader feels pressured to answer "no".

### 5. Convey Character Voice (Narrative)
In first-person narration, rhetorical questions reveal the narrator's emotions — doubt, anger, grief, frustration.

> *"What had I done to deserve this?"*

This communicates the character's anguish far more powerfully than "I was very upset".

## Structural Forms

### One-Word Paragraph Questions
A single rhetorical question on its own line creates maximum impact — one of the one-word/one-line paragraph techniques the BluePrint highlights.

> *Why?*
> 
> The answer, if there was one, never came.

### Clustered Questions (Triples)
Three rapid rhetorical questions build intensity.

> *Was there no end to it? No mercy? No hope at all?*

### Final-Impression Question
Ending a paragraph or whole piece with a rhetorical question leaves the reader thinking.

> *And that — that was the moment I knew I would never be the same. Would you?*

## How to Analyse Rhetorical Questions (Q2/Q3)

### Q2 — Language Analysis
When a source uses a rhetorical question, analyse:

- **Effect**: Does it engage, challenge, or manipulate the reader?
- **Tone**: Is it angry, sorrowful, reflective, sarcastic, triumphant?
- **Position**: At the start (attention-grabbing), middle (transitional), or end (emphatic)?
- **Relationship**: Does the question address the reader directly or seem internal?

> **Example analysis:**
> *"What had become of the world she once knew?" — Atwood*
> 
> Atwood's rhetorical question conveys the narrator's disorientation and grief. By posing it without answer, she forces the reader to contemplate the same loss, deepening our empathy.

### Q3 — Structural Analysis
Rhetorical questions often mark **structural pivots**:

| Position | Structural Function |
|----------|-------------------|
| Opening of text | Hooks the reader immediately |
| Start of paragraph | Signals a new focus or sub-topic |
| Mid-paragraph | Pause for reflection before a reveal |
| End of paragraph | Climax before the next development |
| End of whole text | Memorable closing — lingering effect |

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Too many rhetorical questions | Loses impact, feels gimmicky | Use 1–2 per response max |
| Obvious, pointless questions | "Is the sky blue?" adds nothing | Ask something the reader *wants* to consider |
| Questions that demand an answer | "Are you going to help?" (needs response) | Rephrase: "Who would stand by and do nothing?" |
| No follow-through | Asking then moving on without payoff | Answer or echo the implied answer |

## Exam Strategy (WLV Equivalency)

- **Q5 Writing**: Use **1 rhetorical question** as a structural device — either to open dramatically, to transition mid-piece, or to close with impact.
- **Q2 Language Analysis**: If the source text has a rhetorical question, comment on its effect on the reader and its relationship to the writer's overall purpose.
- **Q3 Structural Analysis**: Identify whether the rhetorical question marks a shift in focus, time, or perspective.

## Golden Words for Rhetorical Questions

- **erotema** (formal term)
- **implied answer** (the unstated response the reader arrives at)
- **direct address** (the question reaches out to the reader)
- **rhetorical force** (the persuasive weight of the question)
- **structural pivot** (the question as a turning point)
