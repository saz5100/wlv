# Hyena Description Language Analysis (Model Q2 Answer)

This page provides a model analysis for Question 2 of the AQA Paper 1 June 2023 examination, focusing on how Yann Martel uses language to describe the hyena's appearance in lines 10–19 of the *Life of Pi* extract.

## Question

Look in detail at this extract, from lines 10 to 19 of the source. How does the writer use language here to describe the hyena's appearance? [8 marks]

## Model Answer

Martel uses a relentless accumulation of **pejorative adjectives** to create an overwhelmingly negative impression of the hyena. The phrase 'ugly beyond redemption' establishes an absolute, almost moral condemnation, while 'shaggy, coarse' and 'bungled mix of colours' suggest something poorly constructed or failed. The comparison of the spots to 'the symptoms of a skin disease' uses a medical simile to evoke disgust and contagion, contrasting sharply with the 'classy ostentation of a leopard's' spots — a deliberate juxtaposition that elevates the leopard while diminishing the hyena.

The writer employs **animalistic and humanising similes** simultaneously. The hyena's head is 'like that of a bear, but suffering from a receding hairline' — the bear simile suggests power, but the humanising detail of a receding hairline undercuts it, making the creature seem pathetic rather than threatening. The ears are 'ridiculously mouse-like', further diminishing the hyena's dignity. The final simile — 'like no dog anyone would want as a pet' — anchors the description in domestic, familiar terms, making the exotic creature seem merely like a failed pet.

Martel's **sentence forms** reinforce the cumulative effect. Short, declarative sentences ('The mouth is forever open and panting. The nostrils are too big. The tail is scraggly and unwagging.') create a staccato rhythm, as if the narrator is ticking off flaws one by one. This contrasts with the longer, more complex opening sentence that establishes the narrator's lack of prejudice before launching into the attack. The final sentence — 'All the parts put together look doglike, but like no dog anyone would want as a pet' — provides a concluding judgement that summarises the entire description.

## Key Techniques Identified

| Technique | Example | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Pejorative adjectives | 'ugly beyond redemption', 'shaggy, coarse' | Creates moral and aesthetic condemnation |
| Simile | 'like the symptoms of a skin disease' | Evokes disgust and contagion |
| Juxtaposition | Leopard's 'classy ostentation' vs hyena's 'bungled mix' | Elevates one animal while diminishing the other |
| Short declarative sentences | 'The nostrils are too big.' | Creates staccato rhythm of accumulating flaws |
| Humanising detail | 'suffering from a receding hairline' | Makes the creature seem pathetic |
