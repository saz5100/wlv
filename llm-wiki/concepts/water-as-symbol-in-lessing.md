# Water as Symbol in Lessing

In *The Grass is Singing*, water functions as a multi-layered symbol, representing both physical relief and the deeper tensions within Mary and Dick's marriage.

## Water as Physical Relief

Mary's use of water is a desperate attempt to alleviate her physical suffering from the oppressive heat. She pours water over herself, seeking a momentary coolness: 'she stood in a basin on the brick floor, pouring it over her'. The water provides a brief, inadequate respite from the 'sapping, undermining waves' of heat.

## Water as Marital Conflict

The scarcity of water becomes a flashpoint for the couple's underlying tensions. Dick's anger at Mary's water usage — 'What, wasting it like that?' — reveals his prioritisation of economic concerns over her comfort. Mary's response — 'I am so hot I can't stand it. I want to cool myself' — highlights her emotional and physical desperation. The argument over water is not really about water; it is about power, empathy, and the failure of communication in their marriage.

## Water as Resource Scarcity

The logistical difficulty of fetching water — 'a driver, and two workers, and two oxen off other work for a whole morning' — underscores the harshness of rural life. Water is not a convenience but a costly resource, and Mary's 'waste' of it is seen by Dick as a betrayal of their shared struggle.

## Water as Emotional Deprivation

The scarcity of water mirrors Mary's emotional deprivation. Just as the land is parched and waiting for rain, Mary is starved of comfort, understanding, and connection. The 'implacable sky' that refuses to yield rain parallels Dick's inability to understand his wife's needs.
