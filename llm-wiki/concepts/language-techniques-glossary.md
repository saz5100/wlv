# Language Techniques Glossary — AQA Paper 1

A comprehensive reference of language techniques tested in AQA Paper 1 Q2. For each technique you need to: (1) **identify** it, (2) **quote** evidence, and (3) **explain the effect** on the reader.

---

## Figurative Language

### Simile
- **Definition:** A comparison using "like" or "as"
- **Example:** "The sea was like a restless animal, heaving and churning"
- **Effect:** Creates a vivid image; the comparison makes the description more accessible and emotionally charged

### Metaphor
- **Definition:** A direct comparison stating something IS something else
- **Example:** "The sea was a living thing that night — black, heaving, hungry"
- **Effect:** Stronger than simile; forces the reader to see the subject in a new, often more intense way

### Extended Metaphor
- **Definition:** A metaphor sustained across multiple sentences or paragraphs
- **Example:** "The city was a beast. Its streets were arteries, its lights were eyes, its roar was a constant, hungry growl."
- **Effect:** Creates a sustained atmosphere; reinforces a single idea or mood throughout the passage

### Personification
- **Definition:** Giving human qualities to non-human things
- **Example:** "The wind howled in fury, rattling the windows like clenched fists"
- **Effect:** Makes the setting feel alive and interactive; can create sympathy, threat, or atmosphere

### Pathetic Fallacy
- **Definition:** Attributing human emotions to nature or the weather
- **Example:** "The sky wept. Rain streamed down the window like tears."
- **Effect:** Mirrors or contrasts a character's emotional state; sets the mood for the scene

## Sound Techniques

### Alliteration
- **Definition:** Repetition of the same initial consonant sound
- **Example:** "The **d**ark, **d**amp **d**ungeon **d**ripped with **d**ampness"
- **Effect:** Creates rhythm, emphasis, or a particular mood (harsh sounds = anger, soft sounds = calm)

### Sibilance
- **Definition:** Repetition of the 's' or 'sh' sound
- **Example:** "The **s**erpent **s**lithered **s**ilently through the **s**traw"
- **Effect:** Creates a hissing, often sinister or threatening atmosphere

### Onomatopoeia
- **Definition:** Words that imitate the sound they describe
- **Example:** "The waves **crashed**, **hissed**, and **roared** against the cliffs"
- **Effect:** Creates an auditory landscape; immerses the reader in the scene

### Assonance
- **Definition:** Repetition of vowel sounds within words
- **Example:** "The **r**ai**n** **f**e**ll** on the **r**oo**f** with a soft **dr**i**p**"
- **Effect:** Creates internal rhythm and musicality; can reinforce mood

### Consonance
- **Definition:** Repetition of consonant sounds within words (not just initial)
- **Example:** "The **b**lack **b**irds **b**egan to **b**eat their **b**roken wings"
- **Effect:** Creates texture and emphasis; slower pace for reflection

## Word-Level Techniques

### Connotation
- **Definition:** The implied meaning or association of a word beyond its dictionary definition
- **Example:** "The **stench** of decay" vs "The **aroma** of flowers" — both describe smell but carry opposite connotations
- **Effect:** Words carry emotional weight; choosing "slimy" vs "smooth" changes how the reader feels about the subject
- *See dedicated page: [[connotations]]*

### Denotation
- **Definition:** The literal, dictionary definition of a word
- **Example:** "Snake" denotes a long, legless reptile; its connotations include danger, deceit, temptation
- **Effect:** Understanding denotation helps you identify when a writer is deliberately choosing words for their connotations rather than their literal meaning

### Semantic Field
- **Definition:** A group of words all related to the same theme or subject
- **Example:** In a storm scene: "**thunder**", "**lightning**", "**gale**", "**torrent**", "**fury**" all belong to the semantic field of violent weather
- **Effect:** Creates a consistent and reinforcing atmosphere; builds the reader's emotional response through accumulated association

### Contrast / Antithesis
- **Definition:** Placing opposite ideas or words together for effect
- **Example:** "It was the **best** of times, it was the **worst** of times"
- **Effect:** Highlights difference; creates tension and emphasis

### Hyperbole
- **Definition:** Deliberate exaggeration for effect
- **Example:** "I've told you a **million** times"
- **Effect:** Conveys strong emotion; adds humour or urgency

### Euphemism
- **Definition:** A mild or indirect word substituted for one considered too harsh
- **Example:** "He **passed away**" instead of "He died"; "**downsizing**" instead of "firing staff"
- **Effect:** Softens reality; can reveal a character's reluctance to face the truth

### Dysphemism
- **Definition:** A harsh or crude word used in place of a neutral one
- **Example:** "The **rat-infested dump**" instead of "The old house"
- **Effect:** Creates disgust, contempt, or strong negative feeling

## Sentence-Level Techniques

### Imperative
- **Definition:** A command or instruction
- **Example:** "**Listen.** **Look.** **Run.** "
- **Effect:** Creates urgency, authority, or direct address to the reader

### Interrogative / Rhetorical Question
- **Definition:** A question that does not expect an answer
- **Example:** "How could anyone forget such a sight?"
- **Effect:** Engages the reader; encourages them to think; can express frustration or wonder

### Exclamatory
- **Definition:** An exclamation showing strong emotion
- **Example:** "What a nightmare this had become!"
- **Effect:** Conveys shock, excitement, anger, or intensity

### Repetition
- **Definition:** Repeating a word or phrase for emphasis
- **Example:** "**Never** give in. **Never** give up. **Never** stop believing."
- **Effect:** Reinforces a key idea; builds rhythm; creates emotional weight

### Tripling / Rule of Three
- **Definition:** Three related words or phrases used together
- **Example:** "We came, we saw, we conquered"; "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"
- **Effect:** Creates rhythm and emphasis; makes the idea memorable and complete

### Listing / Accumulation
- **Definition:** A series of items, often building in intensity
- **Example:** "The room was filled with **cobwebs, dust, rotting wood, and the smell of decay**"
- **Effect:** Builds a detailed picture; can create a sense of overwhelming quantity or intensity

### Juxtaposition
- **Definition:** Placing two contrasting ideas close together
- **Example:** "The **laughing children** played in the **shadow of the factory chimneys**"
- **Effect:** Highlights contrast; draws attention to inequality, irony, or tension

### Oxymoron
- **Definition:** Two contradictory terms placed together
- **Example:** "**Bitter-sweet**", "**deafening silence**", "**living death**"
- **Effect:** Creates a paradox that forces the reader to think more deeply about the subject

## Structural Language Techniques

### Foreshadowing (Language)
- **Definition:** Words or images that hint at future events
- **Example:** "Little did he know, this was the last time he would see his home"
- **Effect:** Creates anticipation and dramatic irony; prepares the reader emotionally for what comes

### Symbolism
- **Definition:** An object, image, or action that represents an abstract idea
- **Example:** A **storm** symbolises chaos or emotional turmoil; a **bird** symbolises freedom
- **Effect:** Adds layers of meaning; allows the writer to convey abstract ideas concretely

### Imagery
- **Definition:** Language that appeals to the five senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)
- **Example:** "The **bitter cold** bit at his cheeks. The **smell of woodsmoke** hung in the air. Somewhere, a **dog barked** in the distance."
- **Effect:** Immerses the reader in the scene; makes the writing vivid and memorable

### Sensory Language
- **Definition:** Specific vocabulary targeting each sense
- **Example:** Sight: "glimmered, shadowy, blinding"; Sound: "crackled, whispered, thundered"; Smell: "pungent, musty, cloying"; Touch: "gritty, clammy, razor-sharp"; Taste: "metallic, bitter, saccharine"
- **Effect:** Creates a fully immersive experience; draws the reader deeper into the text

### Tone
- **Definition:** The writer's attitude toward the subject, conveyed through word choice
- **Example:** Sombre: "The grey sky wept over the silent procession"; Ironic: "What a **wonderful** day to have my car break down"
- **Effect:** Establishes the mood; influences how the reader interprets the events

### Register
- **Definition:** The level of formality in language
- **Example:** Formal: "The individual proceeded to vacate the premises"; Informal: "He just upped and left"
- **Effect:** Reveals social relationships, setting, and character; shifts can indicate changes in mood or power

## How to Use This Glossary for Q2

1. **Read the question** — identify what feeling or idea you're analysing (e.g. "how does the writer create tension?")
2. **Scan the extract** — look for techniques from this glossary that create that effect
3. **Quote** — pick the best 2-3 examples, not everything you find
4. **Name the technique** — be precise: "the writer uses a **simile**..." not "the writer uses a comparison..."
5. **Explain the effect** — don't stop at "this creates tension". Say WHY: "the comparison to a 'trapped animal' emphasises the character's helplessness, making the reader fear for their safety"

> **Model paragraph:** "The writer uses **personification** to create a sense of threat. The wind is described as 'howling in fury', which gives it human aggression and intent. This makes the setting feel actively hostile, forcing the reader to share the character's vulnerability."
