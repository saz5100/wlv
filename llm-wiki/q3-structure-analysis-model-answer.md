# Q3 Structure Analysis -- Grade 9 Model Answer

## The Question Format

Q3 asks you to analyse how the writer has **structured** the text to interest the reader. You need to consider the **whole text**, not just one extract. Worth **8 marks**.

## Structural Features to Analyse

| Feature | What to look for | Effect |
|---------|-----------------|--------|
| Beginnings/endings | How does the text open and close? Cyclical? | Creates resolution or ambiguity |
| Focus shifts | Where does attention move? | Introduces new perspectives |
| Pace changes | Short vs long paragraphs | Controls tension |
| Time shifts | Flashbacks, foreshadowing | Adds depth, creates anticipation |
| Zoom/pan | Close detail vs wide view | Varies intensity |
| Dialogue placement | Where is speech positioned? | Breaks up narrative, reveals character |

## Grade 9 Model Answer

**Question:** You now need to think about the **whole text**. How has the writer structured the text to interest you as a reader?

> The writer structures the text to create a growing sense of unease that culminates in a moment of crisis. The opening establishes a deceptively calm setting -- "the beach was empty, the sea flat and still" -- using long, languid sentences that mirror the peaceful scene. This creates a false sense of security, making the reader underestimate the danger to come.
>
> The writer then introduces a structural **shift** as the narrator's gaze moves from the horizon to "something dark bobbing in the water". The paragraph length contracts sharply here, from six lines to just two: "He squinted. It moved. His blood ran cold." The abruptness of this shift jolts the reader, mirroring the narrator's sudden realisation.
>
> A further structural device is the use of **cyclical structure**: the text opens with the beach at sunrise and closes with the same image, but transformed -- "the empty beach now seemed to mock him". This circular return to the same setting with a completely different emotional resonance shows how the character has been changed by his experience.
>
> Finally, the writer withholds the full revelation until the final paragraph, creating a **delayed climax**. Short, punchy sentences build tension: "Closer. Darker. A hand." The single-word paragraphs force the reader to process information slowly, mirroring the narrator's dawning horror.

## Why This is Grade 9

* Whole text overview -- discusses beginning, middle, end
* Structural terminology -- focus shift, cyclical structure, delayed climax
* Paragraph-level analysis -- contrasts paragraph lengths
* Quotes demonstrating structure -- shows evidence of structural choices
* Effect on reader throughout -- every point explains reader response
* Sophisticated vocabulary -- languid, culminates, deceptively, abruptness

## Common Q3 Mistakes

* Analysing language instead of structure
* Not considering the whole text (only discussing one paragraph)
* Describing the plot instead of structural choices
* Not quoting evidence of structural features
