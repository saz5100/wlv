# Summarisation with Evidence

The University of Worcester English Equivalency test places significant emphasis on summarisation skills. Questions 5 and 6 require candidates to summarise relationships or views in their own words and support their summaries with evidence from the text.

## What Is Summarisation?

Summarisation is the skill of condensing information from a text into a concise, accurate restatement using your own words. It tests:
- **Comprehension**: Understanding the text's meaning
- **Paraphrasing**: Restating ideas without copying
- **Selection**: Identifying the most relevant information
- **Evidence integration**: Supporting your summary with textual references

## The Summarisation Framework

### Step 1: Identify the Focus
- Q5: Relationship between characters (e.g., Jeanette and her mother)
- Q6: A character's views on a topic (e.g., Mrs Winterson's views on being an author)

### Step 2: Scan the Text
- Look for key statements, actions, and dialogue
- Identify patterns in behaviour or attitude
- Note contrasts or contradictions

### Step 3: Paraphrase
- Restate the key ideas in your own words
- Avoid direct quotation — use your own sentence structures
- Maintain the original meaning and tone

### Step 4: Select Evidence
- Choose 2–3 specific quotations or references
- Ensure evidence directly supports your summary
- Use brief, integrated quotations

### Step 5: Connect Evidence to Summary
- Explain how the evidence supports your paraphrase
- Use phrases like 'This is shown when...' or 'For example...'

## Example

**Question**: In your own words, how would you describe the relationship between Jeanette and her mother? How do you know? Refer to evidence from across the whole passage.

**Model response**:
The relationship between Jeanette and her mother is characterised by tension and disapproval. Jeanette's mother is controlling and dismissive of her daughter's choices, particularly her career as a writer. This is evident when she demands that Jeanette 'did not publish' her novel and refuses to have a telephone, making communication difficult. Despite this, Jeanette persists in her ambitions, suggesting a relationship marked by conflict rather than support.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Copying directly**: Use your own words — paraphrasing is essential
- **Vague summaries**: Be specific about the relationship or views
- **No evidence**: Always support your summary with textual references
- **Irrelevant evidence**: Choose evidence that directly supports your point
- **Over-quoting**: Keep quotations brief and integrated
