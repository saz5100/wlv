# Chapter 1: Understanding the WLV English Equivalency Test

## 1.1 What Is the WLV English Equivalency?

The University of Wolverhampton (WLV) English Equivalency test is a **2-hour on-campus written assessment** designed for PGCE applicants who do not hold a GCSE English Language qualification at grade C/4 or above. It is an accepted alternative to GCSE English for teacher training entry.

The test is mapped directly onto **AQA GCSE English Language Paper 1 (8700)** but is streamlined — several components have been removed to fit a condensed 2-hour format.

## 1.2 Assessment Structure

| Section | Component | AQA Equivalent | Marks | Time |
|---------|-----------|----------------|:-----:|:----:|
| A | Explicit retrieval | Q1 | 4 | ~4 min |
| A | Language analysis | Q2 | 8 | ~12 min |
| A | Structural analysis | Q3 | 8 | ~12 min |
| B | Creative/extended writing | Q5 | 25 | ~45 min |
| | **Total** | | **45** | **2 hours** |

### Section A: Reading Skills (25 minutes recommended)

Candidates analyse a **single unseen** 19th, 20th, or 21st-century literary or non-fiction prose extract. Three skills are tested:

- **Q1 — Explicit Retrieval (4 marks):** Skim a specified section of the text and identify 4 distinct, literal facts.
- **Q2 — Language Analysis (8 marks):** Analyse how the writer uses vocabulary, sensory details, and imagery to shape meaning and create effects.
- **Q3 — Structural Analysis (8 marks):** Examine the writer's structural choices across the whole text — how they shift focus, control pace, and organise the narrative.

### Section B: Writing Skills (45 minutes recommended)

Candidates produce **one continuous piece of extended writing** of approximately **400 words** from a **single mandatory prompt**. There is no choice between narrative and descriptive options.

Marks are awarded for:
- **AO5 — Content and Organisation (12 marks):** Clarity of communication, effective structure, engaging content
- **AO6 — Technical Accuracy (13 marks):** Spelling, punctuation, grammar — **50% of the writing marks**

## 1.3 What Is Excluded

The WLV test explicitly removes:

| Component | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| Spoken Language Endorsement | Not part of on-campus written assessment |
| Paper 2 — text synthesis and comparison | Single source extract only |
| Q4 — Evaluation essay (20 marks) | Stripped from condensed format |
| Writing prompt choice | Single fixed thematic prompt only |
| Multiple writing tasks | One extended piece only |

## 1.4 Key Differences from AQA GCSE

| Aspect | AQA GCSE English Language | WLV Equivalency |
|--------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Duration | 1 hour 45 minutes | 2 hours |
| Sources | Paper 1 + Paper 2 (2 texts) | 1 extract only |
| Total marks | 160 (2 papers) | 45 (1 paper) |
| Q4 Evaluation | Yes (20 marks) | No |
| Writing choice | Narrative OR descriptive | Single fixed prompt |
| SPaG weighting | 20% of writing marks | 50% of writing marks |
| Handwriting | No penalty | Legibility matters for marking |

## 1.5 The Mark Scheme at a Glance

### Section A — Reading (Q1–Q3)

| Question | Marks | What the examiner looks for |
|:--------:|:-----:|----------------------------|
| Q1 | 4 | 4 explicit facts from specified lines |
| Q2 | 8 | Analysis of language choices with subject terminology + effect on reader |
| Q3 | 8 | Analysis of structural choices across the whole text + effect on reader |

### Section B — Writing (Q5)

| AO | Marks | Focus |
|:--:|:-----:|-------|
| AO5 | 12 | Content, organisation, paragraphing, tone |
| AO6 | 13 | Spelling, punctuation, grammar, sentence demarcation |

**Critical point:** AO6 (SPaG) accounts for **over half** the writing marks. For a candidate scoring 25/25 on Q5, SPaG alone is worth 13 marks — more than Q2 and Q3 combined.

## 1.6 Grade Boundaries (Approximate)

| Grade | Total (out of 45) |
|:-----:|:------------------:|
| Pass / C equivalent | 22–24 |
| Merit / B equivalent | 28–30 |
| Distinction / A equivalent | 34+ |

These are approximate. Check with WLV for current grade boundaries.

# Chapter 2: Section A — Reading Skills Masterclass

## 2.1 Overview

Section A tests your ability to read and analyse a **single unseen prose extract**. You will answer three questions (Q1, Q2, Q3) on the same text, increasing in complexity from straightforward retrieval to sophisticated structural analysis.

## 2.2 The Single-Source Extraction Principle

Unlike AQA GCSE which uses two separate texts across two papers, the WLV test uses **one extract** for all reading questions. This means:

- You only need to familiarise yourself with **one text**
- Q1, Q2, and Q3 all draw from the same source
- You can build your understanding of the text as you progress through the questions
- There is **no comparison** between different texts

## 2.3 How to Approach the Extract

When you first see the extract, spend **2–3 minutes** doing the following:

**Step 1: Orient yourself**
- Read the title and author (if given) — this tells you the era and genre
- Identify the text type: is it literary fiction, non-fiction, memoir, travel writing?
- Note the line numbers — questions specify exact line ranges

**Step 2: Quick-read the whole extract**
- Read through once to understand the gist
- Identify: Who? What? Where? When?
- Note the mood or atmosphere
- Mark any striking language or structural features (mental note)

**Step 3: Locate the Q1 lines**
- Q1 always specifies a short section (usually lines 1–5 or similar)
- Read this section carefully — you need exact facts from here
- Don't worry about the rest of the text yet for Q1

## 2.4 Time Allocation for Section A

| Question | Recommended time | Marks | Marks per minute |
|:--------:|:---------------:|:-----:|:----------------:|
| Reading time | 3 min | — | — |
| Q1 | 4 min | 4 | 1.0 |
| Q2 | 12 min | 8 | 0.67 |
| Q3 | 12 min | 8 | 0.67 |
| **Total** | **31 min** | **20** | |

## 2.5 What Examiners Are Looking For

Across all three reading questions, examiners reward:

- **Precision** — exact quotes, specific facts, accurate terminology
- **Relevance** — everything you write must directly answer the question
- **Depth over breadth** — for Q2 and Q3, three well-developed points are better than five shallow ones
- **Effect on the reader** — every analysis must explain WHY the writer made that choice and HOW it affects the reader

# Chapter 3: Q1 — Explicit Retrieval (4 Marks)

## 3.1 What Q1 Tests

Q1 assesses your ability to **identify and retrieve explicit information** from a short, specified section of the text. This is the most straightforward question on the paper — it tests whether you can read carefully and extract literal facts without inferring, interpreting, or evaluating.

**Format:** "List four things about [subject] from lines X–Y."

**Marks:** 4 marks (1 mark per correct fact)

## 3.2 The 5-Step Method

| Step | Time | What to Do |
|:----:|:----:|------------|
| 1. Read the question | 30s | Identify exactly what the question asks you to find — who or what is the subject? |
| 2. Locate the lines | 30s | Go directly to the specified line range. Read only that section. |
| 3. Skim for facts | 1 min | Look for concrete, explicit details — actions, descriptions, locations, times |
| 4. Select 4 facts | 1 min | Choose the clearest, most undeniable pieces of information |
| 5. Write them down | 1 min | Use short phrases or bullet points. Full sentences are not required. |

## 3.3 What Counts as a Fact?

A fact is something **explicitly stated** in the text — not implied, not inferred, not guessed.

| ✅ Good facts | ❌ Not facts |
|--------------|-------------|
| "The boy ran towards the house" (stated action) | "The boy was scared" (interpretation) |
| "The door was painted red" (stated description) | "The red door symbolised danger" (analysis) |
| "They were in a narrow alley" (stated location) | "The alley felt claustrophobic" (opinion) |
| "It was early morning" (stated time) | "The morning symbolised a new beginning" (interpretation) |
| "She whispered, 'Help me'" (stated speech) | "She was desperate for help" (inference) |

**Key principle:** If you have to read between the lines, it's not a fact. The answer should be visible on the page.

## 3.4 The 2026 Multiple-Choice Update

From 2026, Q1 has changed to a **multiple-choice format** on AQA papers. You shade circles to select the **4 correct statements** from a list of 8 options.

**Strategy for multiple-choice Q1:**

1. Read each statement carefully
2. Return to the specified lines
3. Tick statements that are **explicitly stated** word-for-word or very close paraphrase
4. Eliminate statements that are:
   - Not mentioned at all
   - Partially true but not fully accurate
   - Inferences or interpretations
   - True of the text generally but not in the specified lines
5. Select exactly 4 — no more, no less

**Common trick:** The examiners include 2–3 statements that sound plausible but are not actually stated. Don't be fooled by "it could be true" — only select what IS stated.

## 3.5 Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Loses Marks |
|---------|-------------------|
| Writing 3 facts instead of 4 | Only get 3 marks out of 4 — always count your points |
| Inferring rather than retrieving | "The character was nervous" when the text says "his hands trembled" — the statement is not explicitly made |
| Writing opinions or evaluations | "The description was vivid" — that's your opinion, not a fact from the text |
| Copying huge chunks of text | Wastes time and may contain multiple facts in one quote — separate them |
| Writing full sentences | Short phrases are faster and clearer |
| Including facts from outside the specified lines | May be ignored or marked wrong |
| Repeating the same fact in different words | Only counts once |

## 3.6 Worked Example

**Extract (lines 1–5):**

> *The Master was a slightly peculiar man. He had travelled extensively abroad and kept a small menagerie of exotic pets. He lived alone in a large, crumbling house at the edge of the village.*

**Question:** List four things about the Master from lines 1–5.

| Answer | Verification |
|--------|-------------|
| 1. He was slightly peculiar | Explicitly stated: "a slightly peculiar man" |
| 2. He had travelled extensively abroad | Explicitly stated: "travelled extensively abroad" |
| 3. He kept exotic pets | Explicitly stated: "kept a small menagerie of exotic pets" |
| 4. He lived alone | Explicitly stated: "He lived alone" |

**Wrong answers that would not get marks:**

- "He was lonely" — not stated (inference)
- "He was rich" — not stated
- "His house was scary" — opinion, not fact

## 3.7 Practice Passages

**Practice 1:**

> *Sarah woke before dawn. The old cottage creaked around her as she pulled on her boots. Rain hammered against the windowpanes. She had promised her grandmother she would feed the chickens, rain or shine.*

**Question:** List four things about Sarah from lines 1–5.

*(Try it yourself before checking the answers below.)*

**Answers:**
1. She woke before dawn
2. She pulled on her boots
3. She had promised her grandmother to feed the chickens
4. She promised she would do it rain or shine

**Practice 2:**

> *The market was alive with noise. Stallholders called out their prices, competing with the squawking of caged chickens. The smell of fresh bread mingled with the sharp tang of fish. A small boy darted between the crowds, clutching a copper coin in his fist.*

**Question:** List four things about the market from lines 1–5.

*(Try it yourself.)*

**Answers:**
1. The market was alive with noise
2. Stallholders called out their prices
3. The smell of fresh bread was there
4. A small boy was clutching a copper coin

# Chapter 4: Q2 — Language Analysis (8 Marks)

## 4.1 What Q2 Tests

Q2 assesses your ability to **analyse how the writer uses language** to achieve effects and influence the reader. You are given a short extract (typically around 12 lines) from the source. This is the first analysis question — it requires you to move beyond what the text says to **how** it says it.

**Format:** "Look in detail at this extract from lines X to Y. How does the writer use language here to [describe/show/convey] [subject]?"

**Marks:** 8 marks

**Time:** ~12 minutes

## 4.2 The WHAT-HOW-WHY Framework

Every point in your Q2 answer should follow this three-part structure:

| Element | What to Do | Example |
|:-------:|-----------|---------|
| **WHAT** | Identify the technique + quote the evidence | "The writer uses a **simile**: 'the sea was a living thing'" |
| **HOW** | Analyse the specific word choices | "The verb 'living' **personifies** the sea, giving it intent and agency" |
| **WHY** | Explain the effect on the reader | "This **makes the reader feel** the sea is an active, unpredictable threat" |

**The golden rule:** Never state a technique without explaining its effect. "The writer uses a simile" gets zero marks unless you say WHY.

## 4.3 How to Structure Your Answer

**Option A — Thematic paragraphs (recommended)**

Group your analysis into 2–3 paragraphs, each focusing on a different aspect of language:

- **Paragraph 1:** Imagery and sensory language (similes, metaphors, personification, sensory details)
- **Paragraph 2:** Word choices and connotations (specific nouns, powerful verbs, adjectives, adverbs)
- **Paragraph 3:** Sentence-level choices (if relevant — short sentences for tension, long sentences for flow)

**Option B — Chronological order**

Work through the extract in order, analysing language features as they appear. This is safer but can feel less sophisticated.

## 4.4 Subject Terminology You Must Know

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Simile** | Comparison using 'like' or 'as' | "Her voice was like a rusty gate" |
| **Metaphor** | Direct comparison stating one thing IS another | "The sea was a living thing" |
| **Personification** | Giving human qualities to non-human things | "The wind howled its fury" |
| **Sensory language** | Words appealing to the five senses | "The acrid smell of smoke" (smell), "The rough stone wall" (touch) |
| **Semantic field** | A group of related words | Words like "menacing", "looming", "threatening" create a semantic field of danger |
| **Connotation** | The emotional association of a word | "Home" connotes warmth and safety; "House" is neutral |
| **Verb** | An action or state | "He **sprinted**" vs "He **walked**" — different speeds |
| **Adjective** | A describing word | "The **dark**, **foreboding** forest" |
| **Adverb** | Describes a verb | "She **quietly** opened the door" |
| **Imagery** | Visually descriptive language | "The sun bled across the horizon" |
| **Oxymoron** | Two contradictory terms together | "Deafening silence" |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Weather reflecting mood | A storm during an argument |
| **Alliteration** | Repeated initial sounds | "The **s**oft **s**and **s**lipped" |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Words that sound like their meaning | "Crash", "hiss", "buzz" |

## 4.5 Level of Response Marking — What Makes Grade 9?

| Level | Description | Marks |
|:-----:|-------------|:-----:|
| 1 | Simple identification of techniques. No analysis of effects. | 1–2 |
| 2 | Some explanation of effects. Attempts at subject terminology. | 3–4 |
| 3 | Clear explanation of effects. Appropriate terminology. Clear focus on question. | 5–6 |
| 4 | Perceptive, detailed analysis. Precise terminology. Effects on reader fully explored. | 7–8 |

**To reach Level 4 (Grade 9):**

- Analyse individual word choices, not just whole phrases
- Show how multiple techniques work **together** to create an overall effect
- Use precise subject terminology confidently
- Every point must link back to the **effect on the reader**
- Use sophisticated analytical phrases (see 4.7)

## 4.6 Grade 9 Model Answer

**Source extract:** Lines 13–25 describing the narrator's fear

**Question:** How does the writer use language here to describe the narrator's fear?

> The writer uses powerful sensory language to convey the narrator's terror. The simile **"my heart hammered against my ribs like a trapped bird"** vividly captures the physical sensation of fear. The verb **"hammered"** suggests violent, uncontrolled pounding, implying the narrator has lost control of his own body. The comparison to a **"trapped bird"** is particularly effective — a bird in a cage evokes desperation and the instinct to escape, which mirrors the narrator's psychological state. Together, these word choices **force the reader to feel the narrator's panic viscerally**, as if experiencing it themselves.
>
> Furthermore, the writer employs **short, fragmented sentences**: "Darkness. Silence. Then — a sound." The full stops create heavy pauses that **slow the reader's pace**, imitating the narrator's hypervigilant state where every second stretches into an eternity. The **dash** before "a sound" creates a **jolt of tension** — the reader, like the narrator, is bracing for impact. This structural technique works alongside the language choices to build an immersive sense of dread.
>
> The **semantic field of danger** is established through carefully chosen adjectives: **"menacing"**, **"looming"**, and **"threatening"**. The word **"menacing"** attributes hostile intent to the surroundings — the environment itself becomes an antagonist. **"Looming"** suggests something vast and unavoidable, creating a sense of entrapment. These words accumulate to create an **atmosphere of inescapable peril**, positioning the reader to share the narrator's helplessness.

**Why this is Grade 9:**

- ✅ Precise subject terminology (simile, verb, semantic field, dash)
- ✅ Embedded quotations — quotes woven naturally into analysis
- ✅ Word-level analysis — explores individual words ("hammered", "menacing", "looming")
- ✅ Effect on reader — every point links back to reader response
- ✅ Comparative analysis — shows how techniques work together
- ✅ Sophisticated phrasing — "viscerally", "positions the reader", "inescapable peril"

## 4.7 Analytical Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to elevate your analysis:

**Introducing evidence:**
- "The writer employs..."
- "Through the use of..."
- "The writer draws on..."
- "A particularly striking example is..."

**Analysing word choices:**
- "The connotation of [word] suggests..."
- "[Word] carries associations of..."
- "The verb/adjective [word] implies..."
- "This contrasts with..."

**Explaining effect:**
- "This forces the reader to..."
- "The reader is positioned to feel..."
- "This creates a sense of..."
- "The effect is to..."
- "This evokes a feeling of..."

**Connecting ideas:**
- "Furthermore..."
- "Similarly..."
- "In contrast..."
- "Building on this..."
- "This is reinforced by..."

## 4.8 Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Costs Marks |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Feature spotting** — listing techniques without analysis | "The writer uses a simile, a metaphor and personification" with no explanation scores 0 |
| **Not quoting** — vague references | "The writer uses imagery" — which imagery? Where? |
| **Quoting too much** — giant chunks of text | Waste of words; pick the key 2–5 words |
| **Telling the story** — describing what happens | "First this happened, then that happened..." — that's not analysis |
| **Vague vocabulary** | "The word creates an effect" — WHAT effect? Be specific |
| **No effect on reader** | Every analysis must end with HOW the reader responds |
| **One long paragraph** | Structure your answer into clear analytical paragraphs |
| **Going outside the specified lines** | The question specifies exact lines — don't stray |

# Chapter 5: Q3 — Structural Analysis (8 Marks)

## 5.1 What Q3 Tests

Q3 assesses your ability to analyse **how the writer has structured the text** to interest the reader. Unlike Q2 which focuses on a short extract, Q3 requires you to consider the **whole text** — its beginning, middle, and end, and how the writer moves between them.

**Format:** "You now need to think about the **whole text**. How has the writer structured the text to interest you as a reader?"

**Marks:** 8 marks

**Time:** ~12 minutes

## 5.2 Structure vs Language — The Critical Difference

This is the most common confusion in the exam. Remember:

| Language (Q2) | Structure (Q3) |
|--------------|----------------|
| Individual words and phrases | How the text is organised as a whole |
| Similes, metaphors, sensory language | Focus shifts, pace changes, time shifts |
| "Why did the writer choose THIS WORD?" | "Why did the writer put this AFTER that?" |
| Word-level analysis | Whole-text analysis |

**If you find yourself analysing individual word choices in Q3, you are answering Q2 and will lose marks.**

## 5.3 Structural Features Glossary

| Feature | What to Look For | Why a Writer Uses It |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Beginnings and endings** | How does the text open? How does it close? | Establishes tone; creates resolution or leaves ambiguity |
| **Cyclical structure** | Does the ending mirror the opening? | Creates a sense of completion or shows change |
| **Focus shifts** | Where does the writer's attention move? (e.g. from setting to character, from external to internal) | Introduces new perspectives, builds complexity |
| **Zoom and pan** | Alternating close-up detail with wide-angle description | Controls intensity; varies reader engagement |
| **Pace changes** | Short paragraphs vs long paragraphs; short sentences vs long sentences | Short = tension, urgency; Long = reflection, atmosphere |
| **Time shifts** | Flashbacks, flash-forwards, chronological jumps | Adds depth, reveals backstory, creates anticipation |
| **Dialogue placement** | Where is speech positioned in the text? | Breaks up narrative, reveals character, changes pace |
| **Paragraph length variation** | Contrast between short and long paragraphs | Short = impact, shock; Long = description, reflection |
| **Withholding information** | Key details revealed gradually | Builds suspense, keeps reader guessing |
| **Climax placement** | Where is the most intense moment? | Peak of tension; often near the end |
| **Juxtaposition** | Contrasting scenes placed next to each other | Highlights differences; creates irony |
| **Repetition of motifs** | Recurring images, ideas or phrases | Creates unity; reinforces themes |

## 5.4 How to Structure Your Answer

A Grade 9 Q3 answer should cover **three structural features** across **three paragraphs**, organised as follows:

**Paragraph 1: The opening**
- How does the writer begin? (Long/short paragraph? Action/description/dialogue?)
- What tone is established?
- How does this hook the reader?

**Paragraph 2: The middle / shifts**
- Where does the focus shift to?
- How does the writer control pace?
- What structural devices are used to maintain interest?

**Paragraph 3: The ending / overall shape**
- How does the text end?
- Is there a cyclical structure, resolution, or cliffhanger?
- How does the structure as a whole affect the reader?

## 5.5 Grade 9 Model Answer

**Question:** You now need to think about the **whole text**. How has the writer structured the text to interest you as a reader?

> The writer structures the text to create a **growing sense of unease** that culminates in a moment of crisis. The opening establishes a **deceptively calm setting** — "the beach was empty, the sea flat and still" — using long, languid sentences that mirror the peaceful scene. This creates a **false sense of security**, making the reader underestimate the danger to come. The opening paragraph is lengthy, inviting the reader to relax into the description, which makes the subsequent shift more jarring.
>
> The writer then introduces a structural **shift** as the narrator's gaze moves from the horizon to **"something dark bobbing in the water"**. The paragraph length **contracts sharply** here, from six lines to just two: "He squinted. It moved. His blood ran cold." This **abrupt contraction** jolts the reader, mirroring the narrator's sudden realisation that something is wrong. The writer has deliberately **withheld the full nature of the threat** until this moment, sustaining the reader's curiosity through the opening section. This zoom-in effect — from wide landscape to a single detail — forces the reader to share the narrator's focus.
>
> A further structural device is the use of **cyclical structure**: the text opens with the beach at sunrise and closes with the same image, but transformed — **"the empty beach now seemed to mock him"**. This circular return to the same setting with a completely different emotional resonance shows how the character has been changed by his experience. The final paragraph echoes the first in length and rhythm, creating **structural symmetry**, but the content is inverted — peace has become menace. This contrast leaves the reader with a lingering sense that nothing is as it first appeared.

**Why this is Grade 9:**

- ✅ Whole-text overview — discusses beginning, middle, and end
- ✅ Precise structural terminology — focus shift, cyclical structure, withholding information, zoom
- ✅ Paragraph-level analysis — contrasts paragraph lengths explicitly
- ✅ Quotes demonstrating structure — shows evidence of structural choices, not just language
- ✅ Effect on reader throughout — every point explains reader response
- ✅ Sophisticated vocabulary — culminates, deceptively, jarring, symmetry, inverted

## 5.6 Level of Response Marking

| Level | Description | Marks |
|:-----:|-------------|:-----:|
| 1 | Simple overview — identifies structural features without analysis | 1–2 |
| 2 | Some explanation of structural choices and effects | 3–4 |
| 3 | Clear, relevant analysis of structure with appropriate terminology | 5–6 |
| 4 | Perceptive, detailed analysis of structural choices. Considers whole text. | 7–8 |

**To reach Level 4:**

- Discuss the **whole text** — beginning, middle, and end
- Use precise structural terminology confidently
- Explain **why** the writer made each structural choice
- Link every point to the **effect on the reader**
- Show how different structural features work together

## 5.7 Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Costs Marks |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Analysing language instead of structure** | This is the #1 error. Save word-level analysis for Q2. |
| **Only discussing one paragraph** | You must discuss the whole text — beginning, middle, end |
| **Describing the plot** | "First this happened, then that happened" — that's summary, not analysis |
| **Not quoting structural evidence** | You need to show the structural feature you're analysing |
| **Vague structural vocabulary** | "The text changes" — HOW does it change? Use precise terms |
| **No effect on reader** | As with Q2, every point must explain reader response |

## 5.8 Practice Questions

**Extract:** A short story that opens with a long description of a peaceful village, shifts to the arrival of a stranger, and ends with the stranger's departure leaving the village changed.

**Question:** How has the writer structured the text to interest you as a reader?

**Plan your answer:**

1. **Opening:** Long descriptive paragraph — establishes normality, creates comfort
2. **Middle:** Shift introduced by stranger's arrival — paragraph shortens, tension builds
3. **Ending:** Cyclical — returns to village scene but transformed — shows impact

Each point should: name the feature → show evidence → explain effect on reader.

# Chapter 6: Section B — Writing Skills Masterclass

## 6.1 Overview

Section B is the **writing section** — worth 25 marks (55% of the total paper). You produce one extended piece of writing of approximately **400 words** in response to a **single fixed prompt**.

This is the most heavily weighted section of the paper. **More than half the marks** for the entire test come from this one question.

## 6.2 The Fixed Thematic Prompt — No Choice

The WLV test uses a **fixed thematic prompt** — you do NOT get a choice between narrative and descriptive writing. You receive one prompt and must respond to it.

**Example prompts:**
- "Write a description of a busy market as suggested by this picture."
- "Write a story about a difficult journey."
- "Describe a place that made you feel uneasy."
- "Write a story about a surprising discovery."

**What this means for your revision:**
- Practise both descriptive and narrative writing — you won't know which
- Practise responding to single prompts without options
- Develop a flexible structure that works for any prompt

## 6.3 The Mark Scheme

| AO | Focus | Marks | Weighting |
|:--:|-------|:-----:|:---------:|
| AO5 | Content and Organisation | 12 | 48% |
| AO6 | Technical Accuracy (SPaG) | 13 | 52% |

**Critical insight:** AO6 (SPaG) is worth MORE marks than AO5. This is the opposite of AQA GCSE where AO5 is worth 24 marks and AO6 is 16. For WLV, SPaG is the single most important component.

### AO5 — Content and Organisation (12 marks)

| Level | Content | Organisation |
|:-----:|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Simple ideas; limited detail | Limited paragraphing; no clear structure |
| 2 | Some developed ideas; relevant | Paragraphs with some structure; clear beginning/end |
| 3 | Consistently developed ideas | Effective paragraphing; logical sequence |
| 4 | Sophisticated ideas; convincing | Coherent whole; sophisticated paragraphing; deliberate structural choices |

### AO6 — Technical Accuracy (13 marks)

| Level | Marks | Criteria |
|:-----:|:-----:|----------|
| 1 | 1–4 | Frequent errors; sentences not always demarcated |
| 2 | 5–8 | Generally correct; some variety in punctuation |
| 3 | 9–11 | Accurate spelling and grammar; range of punctuation used effectively |
| 4 | 12–13 | Highly accurate; full range of punctuation; controlled sentence structures |

## 6.4 Time Management for Q5

| Stage | Time | What to Do |
|:-----:|:----:|------------|
| Planning | 5 min | Brainstorm ideas, choose structure, note vocabulary |
| Writing | 30 min | Aim for 3–4 paragraphs, ~400 words |
| Review | 10 min | Check SPaG, punctuation, paragraphing, sentence openings |

**Total: 45 minutes** — this is the longest section, and rightly so given it carries the most marks.

## 6.5 The CAP Rule

The CAP rule is a memorable way to check your writing before submission:

| Letter | Stands For | What to Check |
|:------:|:----------:|---------------|
| **C** | **Capitalisation** | Capital letters for sentence starts, proper nouns, and the word "I" |
| **A** | **Apostrophes** | Contractions (don't, can't, it's) and possession (the boy's coat, the girls' school) |
| **P** | **Punctuation** | Full stops, commas, question marks — and variety (colons, semicolons, dashes) |

# Chapter 7: Q5 — Creative Writing (25 Marks)

## 7.1 Descriptive vs Narrative Writing

Although you won't have a choice on the day, you need to be prepared for both:

### Descriptive Writing

**Purpose:** To describe a scene, person, or object in vivid detail

**Structure:**
1. **Opening paragraph** — Establish the overall scene using sensory details (sight, sound, smell, touch). Zoom in on one key image to anchor the description.
2. **Middle paragraphs (2–3)** — Explore different aspects of the scene: shift focus from foreground to background, from one sense to another, from stillness to movement. Use the **zoom and pan** technique.
3. **Closing paragraph** — Return to the overall atmosphere. End with a striking image or reflective thought that gives the description unity.

**Key techniques:**
- Sensory language — engage all five senses
- Varied perspectives — zoom in on details, pan out for context
- Similes and metaphors
- Personification of the environment

### Narrative Writing

**Purpose:** To tell a story with a clear sequence of events

**Structure:**
1. **Opening** — Drop the reader straight into action or intrigue. Establish character and setting quickly. Use in medias res (starting in the middle of action).
2. **Build-up** — Introduce conflict or tension. Use short sentences for pace, longer ones for description.
3. **Climax** — The most intense moment. Switch to shorter paragraphs and sensory language. Use sentence fragments for impact.
4. **Resolution** — Show the aftermath. Leave the reader with a lingering image or feeling. Consider a cyclical ending that returns to the opening image.

**Key techniques:**
- In medias res — start in the middle of action
- Show, don't tell — use actions, dialogue, and sensory details
- Vary pace — short sentences for tension; long sentences for atmosphere
- Dialogue — reveals character and breaks up description
- Cliffhangers and twists

## 7.2 The Show, Don't Tell Principle

This is the single most important technique for top marks in Q5.

| Telling (Weak) | Showing (Strong) |
|---------------|------------------|
| "He was scared." | "His heart hammered against his ribs. A cold sweat trickled down his spine." |
| "The room was messy." | "Clothes lay strewn across the floor. A tower of unwashed dishes balanced precariously in the sink." |
| "She was exhausted." | "Her eyelids drooped. Every step felt like wading through treacle." |
| "It was a hot day." | "Heat shimmered off the tarmac. The air was thick and still, cicadas buzzing in the distance." |

**How to show instead of tell:**
- Use **actions**: "He paced the room" not "He was nervous"
- Use **dialogue**: "'I can't do this,' she whispered" not "She was scared"
- Use **sensory details**: "The air tasted of salt and diesel" not "They were near the harbour"
- Use **physical reactions**: "Her hands trembled" not "She was afraid"

## 7.3 Sentence Variety for AO6

To reach the top marks for AO6, you need to demonstrate a **range of sentence structures**:

| Sentence Type | Example | Effect |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| **Simple** | "The door creaked open." | Direct, impactful |
| **Compound** | "The wind howled and the rain lashed against the window." | Connects related ideas |
| **Complex** | "Although he knew it was dangerous, he stepped forward." | Shows sophisticated thinking |
| **Fragment** | "Silence. Darkness. Then — a sound." | Dramatic, punchy |
| **Rhetorical question** | "What choice did he have?" | Engages the reader |

**Also vary your sentence openings:**
- Adverb: "Cautiously, she opened the door."
- Prepositional phrase: "Through the mist, a shape emerged."
- Connective: "Despite the rain, they pressed on."

## 7.4 Punctuation Variety for AO6

For top marks, use a range of punctuation:

| Punctuation | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| **Colon (:)** | "He knew what he had to do: run." |
| **Semicolon (;)** | "The sun was setting; the sky blazed orange." |
| **Dash (—)** | "She took a step back — and froze." |
| **Ellipsis (...)** | "He stared into the darkness, waiting..." |
| **Question mark (?)** | "Could she really do this?" |
| **Exclamation mark (!)** | "Run!" |

## 7.5 Grade 9 Model Answer (Narrative)

**Prompt:** Write a story about a life-saving rescue.

> The sea was a living thing that night — black, heaving, hungry. Liam stood at the water's edge, the spray stinging his face like a thousand needles. Somewhere out there, beyond the roar of the waves, a child was screaming.
>
> He didn't think. He ran.
>
> The cold hit him like a wall, stealing his breath. Each stroke was a battle against the current, his muscles screaming in protest. The lighthouse beam swept across the water, and for one split second, he saw her — a small figure clinging to a capsized boat.
>
> "Hold on!" he roared, the wind ripping the words from his mouth.
>
> She was barely conscious when he reached her, lips blue, eyes half-closed. He grabbed her jacket, wrapped an arm around her chest, and began the desperate swim back. The shore seemed impossibly far. His lungs burned. His legs were lead.
>
> Then — hands. Strong hands pulling them both from the frothing water. The beach. Safety.
>
> Liam collapsed onto the sand, gasping, as the girl coughed up seawater and began to cry. He closed his eyes, letting the rain wash over him. He had never felt so alive.

**What makes this Grade 9:**

| Feature | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Sensory immersion** | "spray stinging his face like a thousand needles" |
| **Short sentences for pace** | "He didn't think. He ran." |
| **Sentence fragments** | "The beach. Safety." |
| **Varied sentence openings** | Atmospheric then action-driven |
| **Powerful verbs** | heaving, roared, ripped, frothing |
| **Sophisticated punctuation** | Dashes, sentence fragments, colons |
| **Emotional arc** | Danger → desperation → relief → reflection |
| **Show, don't tell** | Not "he was brave" but shown through actions |

## 7.6 Grade 9 Model Answer (Descriptive)

**Prompt:** Describe a scene at an abandoned railway station.

> The station was a graveyard of forgotten journeys. Weeds burst through cracks in the concrete platform, their green a defiant slash against the grey. A single bench, its paint peeling like sunburnt skin, faced tracks that had long since fallen silent.
>
> The air tasted of rust and decay. Rainwater pooled on the platform, reflecting a sky the colour of old pewter. Somewhere, a loose sheet of corrugated iron banged rhythmically — a heartbeat in the stillness.
>
> On the far platform, a signal box stood abandoned, its windows smashed, its levers frozen mid-motion as if the signalman had simply vanished mid-shift. Graffiti snaked across its walls — names and dates, small acts of defiance against oblivion.
>
> And yet, there was beauty. A bird had nested in the rusted frame of the station clock. Wildflowers grew between the sleepers. Nature was reclaiming what had been taken. The station was dead, but the world around it was very much alive.

**What makes this Grade 9:**

| Feature | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Extended metaphor** | "graveyard of forgotten journeys" sustains throughout |
| **Sensory variety** | Sight ("green defiant"), taste ("rust"), sound ("banged rhythmically") |
| **Personification** | "Nature was reclaiming what had been taken" |
| **Zoom and pan** | Wide shot (entire station) → close-up (nest in clock) |
| **Juxtaposition** | Decay vs beauty — "And yet, there was beauty" |
| **Sophisticated vocabulary** | defiant, pewter, oblivion, reclaiming |

## 7.7 Planning Template (5 Minutes)

For any prompt, use this 5-minute planning structure:

**Step 1: Brainstorm (1 min)**
- 3–5 key images or moments
- 3 powerful vocabulary words to use
- Opening hook idea

**Step 2: Structure (2 min)**
- Paragraph 1: Opening — hook, establish scene
- Paragraph 2: Development — build tension or explore details
- Paragraph 3: Climax or key moment
- Paragraph 4: Resolution or closing image

**Step 3: Vocabulary upgrade (1 min)**
- Replace weak words: good → magnificent, bad → appalling, big → colossal
- Note 3 similes or metaphors to use

**Step 4: SPaG check plan (1 min)**
- Mark where you'll use colons, semicolons, dashes
- Plan varied sentence openings

## 7.8 Common Mistakes

| Mistake | How to Fix |
|---------|-----------|
| **No plan** | Spend 5 minutes planning — your writing will be more structured and coherent |
| **Writing too little** | Aim for 400 words minimum. Short responses can't reach top marks. |
| **Ignoring paragraphs** | Use paragraphs consistently — at least 3, ideally 4–5 |
| **Repetitive vocabulary** | Use synonyms; build a vocabulary bank in your planning |
| **Telling, not showing** | Replace abstract emotions with concrete actions and sensory details |
| **SPaG errors** | Leave 10 minutes to proofread. Check CAP rule. |
| **Plot that goes nowhere** | Ensure your story has a clear arc — beginning, middle, end |
| **No punctuation variety** | Deliberately use colons, semicolons, and dashes at least once each |
| **Unvarying sentence openings** | Mix adverb, preposition, and connective openings |

# Chapter 8: SPaG Mastery — Punctuation

## 8.1 Why SPaG Matters for WLV

SPaG (Spelling, Punctuation and Grammar) accounts for **52% of the writing marks** — 13 out of 25 marks for Q5. This is the single most important area to practise for the WLV test. A beautifully structured story with poor SPaG will score lower than a simpler story written accurately.

## 8.2 End Punctuation

Every sentence must end with one of these:

| Punctuation | When to Use | Example |
|:-----------:|-------------|---------|
| **Full stop (.)** | End of a statement | "The door was locked." |
| **Question mark (?)** | Direct question or rhetorical question | "What choice did she have?" |
| **Exclamation mark (!)** | Strong emotion, surprise, command | "Run!" |

**Critical rule:** Do not use a comma to join two complete sentences — this is a **comma splice** and is a serious error.

- ❌ "The door was locked, she turned away." (comma splice)
- ✅ "The door was locked. She turned away." (two sentences)
- ✅ "The door was locked, so she turned away." (coordinating conjunction)
- ✅ "Because the door was locked, she turned away." (subordinate clause)

## 8.3 Commas

Commas have several uses:

**Separating items in a list:**
- "I ordered fish, chips, mushy peas and a fizzy drink."
- (Note: the Oxford comma before "and" is optional but be consistent)

**After introductory phrases:**
- "After waiting for an hour, they left without him."
- "Cautiously, she opened the door."

**Around embedded clauses:**
- "Mina, having run the marathon, was exhausted."
- "The house, which had stood empty for years, was finally sold."

**In direct speech:**
- "He shouted, 'Leave me alone!'"
- "'I can't believe it,' she whispered."

**Never use a comma:**
- To join two independent clauses without a conjunction (comma splice)
- ❌ "It was raining, the match was cancelled."
- ✅ "It was raining, so the match was cancelled."

## 8.4 The Comma Splice — Your Biggest Enemy

A comma splice is when you join two complete sentences with only a comma. This is one of the most common and most penalised errors in GCSE writing.

**What is a complete sentence?**
A complete sentence has a subject and a verb and makes sense on its own.

**Examples of comma splices:**
- ❌ "The sun was setting, the sky turned orange."
- ❌ "He walked into the room, everyone turned to look."
- ❌ "I finished my homework, I watched TV."

**How to fix them:**

| Problem | Fix | Example |
|---------|:---:|---------|
| Comma splice | Use a full stop | "The sun was setting. The sky turned orange." |
| Comma splice | Use a semicolon | "The sun was setting; the sky turned orange." |
| Comma splice | Add a conjunction | "The sun was setting, and the sky turned orange." |
| Comma splice | Use a subordinating clause | "As the sun was setting, the sky turned orange." |

## 8.5 Colons and Semicolons

Using these correctly demonstrates sophisticated punctuation and can move you up a grade level.

### Colon (:)

A colon introduces something — an explanation, a list, a quote, or a result.

| Use | Example |
|:---:|---------|
| Before an explanation | "He knew why: the door was locked." |
| Before a list | "Pack the essentials: water, food, a torch and a first aid kit." |
| Before a quote | "She recalled his final words: 'Never give up.'" |
| To show result | "The bridge had collapsed: there was no way forward." |

**Rule:** A colon must be preceded by a complete sentence.

### Semicolon (;)

A semicolon connects two closely related independent clauses without using a conjunction.

| Use | Example |
|:---:|---------|
| Connect related clauses | "The flowers were blooming; the trees were green." |
| Separate complex lists | "The winners were: John, from London; Sarah, from Birmingham; and Tom, from Manchester." |

**Rule:** Both sides of a semicolon must be complete sentences that could stand alone.

**Very common exam technique:** If you're not sure whether to use a colon or semicolon, remember:
- **Colon** = "here comes an explanation/list"
- **Semicolon** = "these two ideas are connected"

## 8.6 Dashes, Brackets and Ellipsis

### Dash (—)

A dash creates a strong break in a sentence.

| Use | Example |
|:---:|---------|
| Interruption in thought | "I finished the meal — if you could call it that — and quickly left." |
| Dramatic pause | "She took a step forward — and stopped." |
| Adding emphasis | "There was only one thing left to do — run." |

**Rule:** Use sparingly. One or two dashes in a piece of writing is effective; more than that looks careless.

### Brackets ( )

Brackets enclose supplementary information.

| Use | Example |
|:---:|---------|
| Extra detail | "A huge man (he was at least seven feet tall) dashed across the road." |
| Clarification | "The meeting (which lasted three hours) was exhausting." |

### Ellipsis (...)

Ellipsis indicates an omission or a trailing-off thought.

| Use | Example |
|:---:|---------|
| Trailing thought | "He stared into the distance, wondering..." |
| Building suspense | "The door creaked open, revealing..." |

**Rule:** Use exactly three dots. No more, no fewer.

## 8.7 Apostrophes

Apostrophes have **two** uses only. Any other use is incorrect.

### 1. Contraction (to show missing letters)

| Contraction | Full form |
|:-----------:|:---------:|
| don't | do not |
| can't | cannot |
| it's | it is / it has |
| you're | you are |
| they're | they are |
| who's | who is |
| I've | I have |
| wouldn't | would not |

### 2. Possession (to show ownership)

| Rule | Example |
|:----:|---------|
| Singular owner: add 's | "the **cat's** tail" (one cat) |
| Plural ending in s: add ' | "the **cats'** tails" (multiple cats) |
| Plural not ending in s: add 's | "the **children's** toys" |
| Singular name ending in s: add 's | "**James's** house" (usually) |

### Critical: Its vs It's

This is the most common apostrophe error in GCSE writing.

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|:----:|:-------:|---------|
| **It's** | It is / It has | "**It's** raining outside." / "**It's** been a long day." |
| **Its** | Belongs to it | "The dog wagged **its** tail." |

**Memory trick:** If you can say "it is" or "it has", use **it's**. Otherwise, use **its**.

## 8.8 Direct Speech Punctuation

**Basic rules:**
- Use speech marks (' ' or " ") around the spoken words
- Start speech with a capital letter
- Punctuation goes INSIDE the closing speech mark
- Start a new paragraph for each new speaker

**Examples:**
- "'Never again!' she cried."
- "He asked, 'Are you coming?'"
- "'I don't know,' she replied, 'but I'll find out.'"

**Common error:** Missing comma before the opening speech mark.
- ❌ "He said "Hello"" 
- ✅ "He said, 'Hello'"

## 8.9 Punctuation Checklist for Self-Review

Before submitting, check:

- ❓ Does every sentence end with a full stop, question mark or exclamation mark?
- ❓ Have I avoided comma splices?
- ❓ Have I used at least one colon or semicolon?
- ❓ Have I used apostrophes correctly — especially its/it's?
- ❓ Is my direct speech punctuated correctly?
- ❓ Have I varied my sentence lengths and types?
- ❓ Are there any missing capital letters?

# Chapter 9: SPaG Mastery — Grammar and Spelling

## 9.1 Sentence Types

You need to demonstrate a range of sentence structures for top marks.

### Simple Sentence

One independent clause (one subject + one verb).

- "The door creaked open."
- "He ran."
- "Silence filled the room."

**Effect:** Direct, impactful, good for key moments.

### Compound Sentence

Two independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).

- "The wind howled **and** the rain lashed against the window."
- "She wanted to leave **but** she couldn't move."

**Effect:** Connects related ideas, shows relationships.

### Complex Sentence

One independent clause + at least one subordinate clause.

- "**Although he knew it was dangerous**, he stepped forward."
- "The man **who lived next door** was a retired teacher."

**Effect:** Shows sophisticated understanding of clause relationships.

### Sentence Fragment

An incomplete sentence used deliberately for effect.

- "Silence. Darkness. Then — a sound."
- "A single word: no."

**Effect:** Creates drama, pace, and impact. Use sparingly.

## 9.2 Parts of Speech Quick Reference

| Part of Speech | Function | Examples |
|:--------------:|----------|:--------:|
| **Noun** | Names a person, place, thing or idea | boy, London, table, courage |
| **Verb** | Describes an action or state | run, is, seem, become |
| **Adjective** | Describes a noun | dark, beautiful, enormous |
| **Adverb** | Describes a verb, adjective or other adverb | quickly, very, silently |
| **Pronoun** | Replaces a noun | he, she, it, they, we |
| **Preposition** | Shows relationship between words | in, on, at, under, through, between |
| **Conjunction** | Connects words or clauses | and, but, or, because, although |
| **Determiner** | Introduces a noun | the, a, an, this, that, my, your |

## 9.3 Common Grammatical Errors

### Subject-Verb Agreement

The subject and verb must agree in number.

- ✅ "The **dog runs** fast." (singular)
- ✅ "The **dogs run** fast." (plural)
- ❌ "The team **are** playing well." — In UK English, collective nouns can take plural verbs, but be consistent.

### Tense Consistency

Do not switch between past and present tense within your writing.

- ❌ "**He walked** into the room and **sees** a shadow." (past → present)
- ✅ "**He walked** into the room and **saw** a shadow." (past → past)
- ✅ "**He walks** into the room and **sees** a shadow." (present → present)

### Misplaced Modifiers

Place descriptive phrases next to what they describe.

- ❌ "**Running for the bus**, his bag fell." (The bag wasn't running)
- ✅ "**Running for the bus**, he dropped his bag."

### Double Negatives

Avoid using two negatives in the same clause.

- ❌ "I didn't do nothing wrong."
- ✅ "I didn't do anything wrong."

## 9.4 Homophones — Most Tested

Homophones are words that sound the same but have different spellings and meanings. These are tested heavily in GCSE English.

| Homophones | Meaning 1 | Meaning 2 |
|:----------:|:----------|:----------|
| **there / their / they're** | Place ("over there") | Belongs to them ("their house") / They are ("they're coming") |
| **your / you're** | Belongs to you ("your book") | You are ("you're right") |
| **its / it's** | Belongs to it ("its tail") | It is ("it's raining") |
| **to / too / two** | Towards ("go to") | Also/ excessively ("too much") / Number ("two dogs") |
| **affect / effect** | Verb: to influence | Noun: the result |
| **accept / except** | Verb: to receive | Preposition: excluding |
| **who's / whose** | Who is ("who's there?") | Belongs to whom ("whose coat?") |
| **where / wear / we're** | Place ("where?") | To have on ("wear clothes") / We are ("we're late") |
| **past / passed** | Time gone by ("in the past") | Went by ("passed the test") |
| **aloud / allowed** | Out loud ("read aloud") | Permitted ("allowed in") |
| **practice / practise** | Noun ("football practice") | Verb ("to practise") |
| **desert / dessert** | Arid land / to abandon | Sweet course after a meal |

The WLV test uses **UK English spelling**. This matters for SPaG marks.

| UK Spelling | US Spelling |
|:-----------:|:-----------:|
| colour | color |
| centre | center |
| organise | organize |
| analyse | analyze |
| defence | defense |
| programme | program |
| travelled | traveled |
| labelled | labeled |
| realise | realize |
| honour | honor |
| behaviour | behavior |
| practise (verb) | practice |

## 9.6 Capitalisation Rules

| Rule | Correct | Incorrect |
|:----:|:-------:|:---------:|
| Start of a sentence | "**T**he door opened." | "**t**he door opened." |
| Proper nouns (names) | "**J**ohn **S**mith" | "**j**ohn **s**mith" |
| Place names | "**L**ondon, **E**ngland" | "**l**ondon, **e**ngland" |
| Days and months | "**M**onday, **J**anuary" | "**m**onday, **j**anuary" |
| The pronoun "I" | "**I** thought **I** saw it" | "**i** thought **i** saw it" |
| Titles | "**M**r, **D**r, **L**ady" | "**m**r, **d**r, **l**ady" |

**Common error:** Capitalising common nouns for emphasis.
- ❌ "It was a **Big** mistake." 
- ✅ "It was a big mistake."

## 9.7 Spelling Strategies

**Commonly misspelled words for GCSE writing:**

| Correct | Common Mistake |
|:-------:|:--------------:|
| definitely | definately |
| separate | seperate |
| embarrass | embarass |
| accommodate | accomodate |
| necessary | neccessary |
| occurrence | occurance |
| occasionally | occassionally |
| privilege | privelege |
| receive | recieve |
| believe | beleive |
| environment | enviroment |
| government | goverment |
| explanation | explaination |
| responsibility | responsability |

**Memory tricks:**
- **definitely** — "finite" is inside it (de-FINITE-ly)
- **separate** — "a rat" is in the middle (sep-A-RAT-e)
- **necessary** — one collar, two sleeves (one c, two s's)
- **accommodate** — two cots, two mattresses (two c's, two m's)
- **believe / receive** — "i before e except after c" (believe = i before e; receive = except after c)

# Chapter 10: Exam Strategy and Practice

## 10.1 Complete Time Plan for the 2-Hour Test

| Phase | Time | Activity | Marks |
|:-----:|:----:|----------|:-----:|
| **Reading** | 3 min | Read the extract. Orient yourself. Identify text type and key features. | — |
| **Q1** | 4 min | Read specified lines. Select 4 explicit facts. Write them as bullet points. | 4 |
| **Q2** | 12 min | Analyse language in specified extract. 3 paragraphs (WHAT-HOW-WHY). | 8 |
| **Q3** | 12 min | Analyse structure across the whole text. 3 paragraphs (beginning/middle/end). | 8 |
| **Section B plan** | 5 min | Plan your writing response. Brainstorm, structure, vocabulary bank. | — |
| **Q5 writing** | 30 min | Write ~400 words. Follow your plan. Use varied sentences and punctuation. | 25 |
| **Q5 review** | 10 min | Check SPaG: CAP rule, homophones, sentence demarcation. | — |
| **Buffer** | 4 min | Review all answers. Add missing detail. Check you've answered all parts. | — |
| **Total** | **120 min** | | **45** |

## 10.2 Handwritten Response Strategies

The WLV test is **handwritten on campus**. This introduces challenges that don't exist in typed exams.

**Before the test:**
- Practise handwriting at speed — set a timer and write continuously for 30 minutes
- Work on legibility — if the examiner can't read it, they can't mark it
- Use a pen you're comfortable with — bring a spare

**During the test:**
- Write on every other line — leaves space for corrections
- Cross out errors with a single line — don't scribble them out
- Leave margins for additions
- If you make a mistake, cross it out neatly and continue
- Write clearly — 'a' and 'o' should be distinguishable, 'n' and 'u' distinct

**For Section B:**
- Use bullet points in your plan, not in your final answer
- Indent paragraphs consistently
- Leave 5 minutes for proofreading — read what you wrote, not what you think you wrote

## 10.3 What to Do When Stuck

| Problem | Solution |
|:--------|:---------|
| Can't find 4 facts for Q1 | Read the specified lines again slowly. Look for actions, descriptions, locations, times. |
| Can't identify a language technique | Focus on individual words and their connotations. You don't always need fancy terminology. |
| Can't see the structure | Think about how the text begins vs ends. Is there a time shift? A change in focus? |
| Don't know what to write for Q5 | Go back to basics: describe the weather, a character, a setting. Something will come. |
| Running out of time | Finish the sentence you're on and move to the next question. Partial marks are better than none. |
| Can't remember spelling | Use a different word. Don't waste time struggling — choose a synonym you can spell. |

## 10.4 Common Mistakes by Question

### Q1 Mistakes
- ❌ Writing only 3 facts — always count to 4
- ❌ Inferring rather than retrieving — stick to what's explicitly stated
- ❌ Writing facts from outside the specified lines

### Q2 Mistakes
- ❌ Feature spotting without analysis — "The writer uses a simile" is not analysis
- ❌ No embedded quotations — "the writer uses imagery" — which imagery?
- ❌ Telling the story instead of analysing language

### Q3 Mistakes
- ❌ Analysing language instead of structure — save word-level analysis for Q2
- ❌ Only discussing one paragraph — must discuss whole text
- ❌ Describing the plot — "first this happened, then that happened"

### Q5 Mistakes
- ❌ No plan — aimless writing that loses coherence
- ❌ Writing too little — aim for 400 words minimum
- ❌ Ignoring SPaG — worth 13 marks, more than Q2 and Q3 combined
- ❌ No punctuation variety — use colons, semicolons, dashes deliberately

## 10.6 Final Words of Advice

1. **SPaG is everything.** With 52% of Q5 marks going to technical accuracy, a grammatically perfect but simple story will outscore a creative story with errors. Prioritise accuracy.

2. **Practise against the clock.** The 2-hour format is tight. Practise each question individually with a timer, then do full mock papers.

3. **Know the difference between language and structure.** This single confusion costs more marks than anything else. Q2 = individual words. Q3 = how the text is organised.

4. **Use past AQA Paper 1 papers.** Since WLV maps onto AQA Paper 1 (Q1, Q2, Q3 only), the best practice is AQA past papers. Download them, practise Q1–Q3, and ignore Q4.

5. **Read your work aloud.** This is the best way to catch SPaG errors. Your ear will hear what your eye misses.

6. **Handwriting practice matters.** If the examiner can't read it, they can't mark it. Practise writing at speed without losing legibility.

7. **Don't panic.** The WLV test is deliberately condensed. There's less content than a full GCSE paper. Focus, pace yourself, and trust your preparation.
