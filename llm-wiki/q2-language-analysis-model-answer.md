# Q2 Language Analysis -- Grade 9 Model Answer

## The Question Format

Q2 asks you to analyse how the writer uses **language** to achieve effects. You are given a short extract (around lines 13-25 of the source). Worth **8 marks**.

## The WHAT-HOW-WHY Framework

| Element | What to do | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **WHAT** | Identify the technique + quote | "The writer uses simile: 'the sea was a living thing'" |
| **HOW** | Analyse the word choices | "The verb 'living' personifies the sea, suggesting it has intent and agency" |
| **WHY** | Explain the effect on reader | "This makes the reader feel the sea is a dangerous, active threat" |

## Grade 9 Model Answer

**Question:** Look in detail at this extract from lines 13 to 25 of the source.

How does the writer use language here to describe the narrator's fear?

> The writer uses powerful sensory language to convey the narrator's terror. The simile "my heart hammered against my ribs like a trapped bird" vividly captures the physical sensation of fear. The verb "hammered" suggests violent, uncontrolled pounding, while the comparison to a "trapped bird" implies desperation and the instinct to escape. This combination forces the reader to feel the narrator's panic viscerally.
>
> Furthermore, the writer employs short, fragmented sentences -- "Darkness. Silence. Then -- a sound." -- to mirror the narrator's fractured thought process under stress. The full stops create pauses that slow the reader down, imitating the narrator's hypervigilant state where every second stretches. The dash before "a sound" creates a jolt of tension.
>
> The semantic field of danger is established through words such as "menacing", "looming", and "threatening". The adjective "menacing" attributes hostile intent to the surroundings, while "looming" suggests something vast and unavoidable. Together, they create an atmosphere of inescapable dread.

## Why This is Grade 9

* Precise subject terminology -- simile, semantic field, fragmented sentences
* Embedded quotations -- quotes woven into the analysis, not just listed
* Word-level analysis -- explores individual word choices
* Effect on reader -- every point links back to reader response
* Comparative analysis -- shows how techniques work together
* Sophisticated phrasing -- "positions the reader to share"

## Common Q2 Mistakes

* Feature spotting (listing techniques without analysis)
* Not quoting from the correct lines
* Describing what happens instead of analysing language
* Using vague terms like "the word creates an effect"
