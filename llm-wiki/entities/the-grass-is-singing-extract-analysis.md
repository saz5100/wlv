# *The Grass is Singing* Extract Analysis (June 2024)

This page provides a detailed analysis of the extract from *The Grass is Singing* used in the AQA June 2024 Paper 1 exam. The extract is ideal for practising Q2 (language analysis) and Q3 (structural analysis).

## Language Analysis (Q2)

### Sensory Language
The extract is rich in sensory detail, particularly auditory and tactile imagery:

- **Sound:** 'the cicadas shrilled incessantly' — the verb 'shrilled' conveys a piercing, irritating noise that mirrors Mary's headache and psychological state. The dogs are heard 'panting softly, or whining with exasperation because of the flies', creating a soundscape of discomfort.
- **Touch/Heat:** 'the heat became intolerable', 'her limbs were heavy and tense', 'the sapping, undermining waves' — heat is described as a physical, oppressive force that drains energy.
- **Sight:** 'heat lizards, vivid red and blue and emerald, darted over the rocks like flames' — the simile compares the lizards to flames, reinforcing the theme of heat. The 'great massed white cloud, like blocks of glittering crystal quartz' creates a visual contrast of beauty and implacability.

### Personification
- **Heat-waves:** 'beat up out of the hot stone' and 'beat down from the iron roof' — the heat is active, aggressive, and inescapable.
- **The sky:** 'implacable sky' — the sky is personified as unyielding and indifferent to human suffering.
- **The brick floor:** 'hissed with dryness' — the brick is given a sound of thirst, anthropomorphising the environment.

### Lexical Fields
- **Heat:** 'heat', 'hot', 'heat-waves', 'flames', 'lukewarm', 'dryness', 'implacable'.
- **Water/Relief:** 'water', 'rain', 'basin', 'bath', 'gallons', 'cool'.
- **Nature/Environment:** 'bush', 'cicadas', 'boulders', 'lizards', 'dogs', 'oxen', 'cloud', 'sky', 'seasons'.

## Structural Analysis (Q3)

### Focus Shifts
The extract moves through a clear sequence of focus shifts:

1. **Internal (Mary's body):** 'Mary's head ached; her limbs were heavy and tense' — the opening focuses on Mary's physical sensations.
2. **External (the environment):** 'Outside in the bush the cicadas shrilled' — the focus shifts to the external world.
3. **Internal (Mary's actions):** She gets water, pours it over herself — the focus returns to Mary's attempts to cope.
4. **Dialogue (Mary and Dick):** The conflict over water usage — the focus shifts to interpersonal tension.
5. **Internal (Mary's thoughts):** 'She was furious' — the focus returns to Mary's emotional state.
6. **Resolution (apology):** 'he apologising, blaming himself, and she forgiving him' — the extract ends with a cyclical pattern of reconciliation.

### Chronological Structure
The extract follows a linear, chronological progression: the heat builds, Mary attempts to cool herself, Dick confronts her, they argue, and they reconcile. This straightforward structure mirrors the oppressive, inescapable rhythm of the seasons.

### Contrast and Juxtaposition
- **Town vs. Farm:** Mary reflects that in town 'there had been no seasons, really', whereas on the farm 'body and mind were subservient to the slow movement of the seasons'.
- **Mary vs. Dick:** Mary's emotional, physical response to heat contrasts with Dick's practical, economic concerns.
- **Inside vs. Outside:** The oppressive heat inside the house ('sapping, undermining waves that beat down from the iron roof') contrasts with the vivid, dangerous beauty of the outside ('heat lizards, vivid red and blue and emerald').

### Cyclical Pattern
The extract ends with a cycle of conflict and apology: 'he apologising, blaming himself, and she forgiving him'. This suggests a dysfunctional pattern rather than genuine resolution, reinforcing the theme of marital tension.

## Themes

- **Environmental oppression:** The natural environment dominates and subjugates the characters.
- **Marital conflict over resources:** The argument over water reveals power dynamics and emotional distance.
- **Psychological deterioration:** Mary's obsession with heat signals her declining mental state.
- **Alienation:** Mary is disconnected from the rhythm of rural life and from her husband.

## Exam Tips

- For **Q2**, focus on the sensory language and personification. Use the [[star-analysis-framework]] to structure your response.
- For **Q3**, trace the focus shifts and the cyclical pattern. Use the [[structure-quoi-comment-pourquoi]] framework.
- The extract is an excellent example of [[third-person-limited-narration]] — the reader is confined to Mary's perspective, creating sympathy for her.
