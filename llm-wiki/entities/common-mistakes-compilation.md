# Common Mistakes Compilation

This page synthesises common mistakes identified from three sources in the [[search-lan-research-log]]: First Rate Tutors, Tutopiya, and Richard Lander School. These mistakes are directly applicable to WLV Equivalency test preparation.

## Sources

- **First Rate Tutors:** 'Common Mistakes in English Language Paper 1' (Substack article)
- **Tutopiya:** 'AQA GCSE English Language Common Mistakes List'
- **Richard Lander School:** 'AQA Language Supporting Booklet 2024' (includes sentence stems and tips)

## Common Mistakes by Question

### Q1 — Explicit Retrieval
- **Quoting too much:** Copying whole sentences instead of selecting specific details.
- **Inferring rather than retrieving:** Adding interpretation when the question asks only for explicit information.
- **Not reading the line references:** Answering based on the whole text rather than the specified lines.

### Q2 — Language Analysis
- **Feature-spotting without analysis:** Naming a device (e.g., 'simile') without explaining its effect.
- **Retelling the story:** Describing what happens instead of analysing how language creates meaning.
- **Weak evidence selection:** Choosing quotations that do not support the analytical point.
- **Ignoring connotations:** Failing to explore the associated ideas and feelings of specific word choices.
- **Formulaic structure:** Using 'The writer uses... which suggests...' without variation.

### Q3 — Structure Analysis
- **Confusing structure with language:** Analysing word choices instead of narrative focus shifts and structural devices.
- **Ignoring the whole text:** Focusing on one paragraph rather than tracing changes across the extract.
- **Listing structural features:** Naming devices (e.g., 'flashback', 'cliffhanger') without explaining their effect on the reader.
- **Not using structural terminology:** Describing what happens without using terms like 'focus shift', 'juxtaposition', or 'cyclical structure'.

### Q5 — Creative Writing
- **Ignoring the prompt:** Writing a pre-prepared story that does not address the specific question.
- **Poor planning:** Starting without a clear structure, leading to rambling or incomplete responses.
- **Weak openings:** Beginning with clichés ('It was a dark and stormy night...') or irrelevant description.
- **Overcomplicating plot:** Trying to fit too many events into 400 words.
- **Neglecting SPaG:** Losing marks due to comma splices, incorrect apostrophes, and poor sentence demarcation.
- **Inconsistent tense:** Switching between past and present tense without reason.
- **Telling instead of showing:** Using abstract statements ('She was scared') instead of sensory detail ('Her hands trembled as she gripped the cold metal railing').

## General Mistakes
- **Poor time management:** Spending too long on one question and rushing others.
- **Not reading the question carefully:** Misinterpreting what is being asked.
- **Illegible handwriting:** Affecting examiner ability to award marks.
- **Not using the source text:** For reading questions, failing to reference the extract.

## WLV-Specific Adaptations

These mistakes are drawn from AQA resources but are equally relevant to the WLV Equivalency test. WLV candidates should pay particular attention to:
- **Q5 SPaG weighting:** 50% of writing marks are allocated to technical accuracy in the WLV test — SPaG mistakes are therefore more costly.
- **Single-source extraction:** All reading questions use one extract; mistakes in Q1 (not reading line references) are especially important.
- **Fixed thematic prompt:** There is no writing choice; ignoring the prompt is a critical error.
