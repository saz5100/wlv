# Comma Splicing

Comma splicing is a significant grammatical error where a comma is used to separate two independent clauses that could each stand as a complete sentence. It is cited by OCR as one of the two most irritating mistakes for GCSE examiners.

## The Effect of Comma Splicing
From a stylistic perspective, comma splicing disrupts the natural rhythm of writing. It removes the necessary pauses provided by full stops, which can leave the reader feeling "breathless" and the prose feeling disjointed.

## Example from *Great Expectations*
The OCR guide references Charles Dickens' *Great Expectations* to illustrate how a series of commas without full stops can create a specific, breathless effect. While this may be a deliberate stylistic choice by a master novelist, in an exam context, it is typically viewed as a failure of [[sentence-demarcation]].

## How to Fix a Comma Splice
To correct a comma splice, a writer can:
1. Replace the comma with a **full stop** (creating two separate sentences).
2. Replace the comma with a **semi-colon** (linking two closely related independent clauses).
3. Replace the comma with a **colon** (if the second clause explains or expands upon the first).
4. Add a **coordinating conjunction** (e.g., 'and', 'but', 'so') after the comma.
