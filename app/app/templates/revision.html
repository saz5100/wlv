📘 Language Analysis

Key language techniques and how to analyse them in the exam

Lang Language Techniques — Complete Guide

📝 Key Language Techniques

TechniqueDefinitionExample🔑 Effect
SimileComparison using 'like' or 'as'"The fog crept like a silent grey blanket"🎨 Creates vivid image; helps reader visualise
MetaphorDirect comparison (one thing IS another)"The sunset was a masterpiece"💥 Powerful, direct image; suggests deeper meaning
PersonificationGiving human qualities to non-human things"The wind howled with icy fingers"👻 Makes abstract feel alive and threatening
ImageryLanguage that appeals to the senses"The air was thick with spices"🌍 Creates vivid sensory experience
AlliterationRepetition of initial consonant sounds"The sly, slithering snake"🎵 Creates rhythm; draws attention to key words
OnomatopoeiaWords that sound like their meaning"Tap, tap, tap of rain"👂 Creates auditory effect; makes description vivid
OxymoronTwo contradictory terms together"Deafening silence"😲 Creates surprise; highlights complexity
HyperboleDeliberate exaggeration"I've told you a million times"📢 Emphasises point; creates humour or drama
Pathetic FallacyWeather reflecting mood"The storm raged as he wept"🌧️ Creates atmosphere; mirrors emotions
Rhetorical QuestionQuestion expecting no answer"How could anyone forget?"🤔 Engages reader; makes them think

📝 How to Analyse Language (PEEL)

  1. P — Point: State the technique the writer uses
  2. E — Evidence: Quote the specific words/phrase
  3. E — Explain: Explain what the technique suggests and its effect on the reader
  4. L — Link: Link back to the overall meaning or purpose of the text

💡 Analysis Sentence Starters

  • "The writer uses a simile to compare... to..., suggesting that..."
  • "The metaphor of... creates the impression that..."
  • "The personification of... makes the reader feel..."
  • "The sensory language of... appeals to the sense of..., creating a vivid image of..."
  • "The alliteration of the '...' sound draws attention to..., emphasising..."
🧠 Mnemonic: Language Techniques

S M P I A O O H P R — Simile, Metaphor, Personification, Imagery, Alliteration, Onomatopoeia, Oxymoron, Hyperbole, Pathetic Fallacy, Rhetorical Question
"Some Monkeys Play In A Old Oak House, Please Remember"

🎯 Exam tip: For language analysis questions (usually 6-8 marks), you need to analyse at least three different techniques in detail. Use the PEEL structure for each one. Never just name the technique — always explain the effect on the reader.
Struct Structural Devices — Complete Guide

🏗️ Key Structural Devices

DeviceDefinition🔑 Effect on Reader
Cyclical StructureEnding mirrors the beginning🔄 Creates completeness or inevitability
FlashbackShift to an earlier time⏪ Provides context; creates contrast with present
ForeshadowingHints at future events🔮 Builds tension; creates dramatic irony
PaceSpeed of the narrative🏃 Short sentences = fast (panic). Long = slow (reflection)
Sentence Length VariationMixing short and long sentences🎵 Creates rhythm; short = impact; long = detail
ParagraphingOrganisation of ideas📄 Short paragraphs = emphasis; long = development
Zoom In/OutWide view to close detail (or vice versa)🎬 Cinematic effect; helps visualise scene
Tricolon (Listing)Three items listed together📋 Creates rhythm; builds intensity
JuxtapositionContrasting ideas placed close together⚡ Highlights differences; creates tension
EllipsisTrailing off with ...💭 Creates suspense; suggests something unsaid

📝 How to Analyse Structure

  • At the start: How does the writer open the text? What is the reader's first impression?
  • In the middle: How does the focus shift? Are there time changes, perspective changes, or changes in pace?
  • At the end: How does the writer conclude? Does the ending echo the beginning (cyclical)?
  • Overall: Why has the writer organised the text in this particular way?

💡 Structure Analysis Sentence Starters

  • "The writer uses a cyclical structure by beginning and ending with..., which suggests that..."
  • "The flashback to... provides context for the reader, showing us why..."
  • "The short, punchy sentences at this point create a sense of urgency, mirroring the character's..."
  • "The writer shifts focus from the wide view of... to the close detail of..., which..."
  • "The juxtaposition of... and... highlights the contrast between..."
🧠 Mnemonic: Structural Devices

C F F P S P Z T J E — Cyclical, Flashback, Foreshadowing, Pace, Sentence length, Paragraphing, Zoom, Tricolon, Juxtaposition, Ellipsis
"Can't Find Fresh Pizza So Please Zoom To Just Eat"

🎯 Exam tip: Structure questions often ask you to analyse the beginning, middle, and end of a text. Always consider why the writer has chosen a particular structure and how it affects the reader's experience. Don't just describe what happens — explain the effect.
Writing Creative Writing — Techniques & Frameworks

✍️ Descriptive Writing — The Senses Framework

SenseQuestions to AskExample
👁️ SightWhat colours, shapes, movements, light?"The sky was a bruised purple."
👂 SoundWhat can be heard? Loud, quiet, rhythmic?"The wind shrieked through the alley."
👃 SmellWhat odours are in the air?"The air smelt of salt and ozone."
✋ TouchWhat textures, temperatures, sensations?"The rain was cold and sharp on her skin."
👅 TasteWhat tastes are present?"She could taste the salt on her lips."

✍️ Narrative Writing — Story Structure

Opening Hook the reader — start in the middle of action (in medias res), with a question, or with vivid description. Establish character, setting, and mood quickly. Build-up Develop the situation. Introduce conflict or tension. Use description to create atmosphere. Vary sentence length to control pace. Climax The turning point — highest tension. Use short sentences for impact. Focus on character's emotions and sensory details. Resolution Show the outcome. Reflect on what has happened. Consider a cyclical ending that echoes the opening.

✍️ Show, Don't Tell

Telling (Weak)Showing (Strong)
"He was angry.""His fists clenched. His jaw tightened. He spoke through gritted teeth."
"She was nervous.""She wiped her clammy hands on her jeans for the third time."
"It was a scary place.""The shadows seemed to move, and every creak of the floorboards made her heart stop."
"He was happy.""A smile spread across his face, and he couldn't stop humming."
🧠 Mnemonic: Descriptive Writing Checklist

S S S S T — Sight, Sound, Smell, Sensation (touch), Taste
"Use all five senses to create a vivid, immersive description."

🎯 Exam tip: For creative writing, spend 5 minutes planning before you write. Decide on your structure (opening → build-up → climax → resolution) and which techniques you will use (simile, metaphor, personification, sensory language). Quality over quantity — a well-crafted paragraph is better than a rushed page.
SPaG Spelling, Punctuation & Grammar

🔤 Punctuation Guide

MarkUsageExample
Full Stop .Ends a complete sentenceThe sun was shining.
Comma ,Separates items in a list; after fronted adverbials; before conjunctionsFirst, I went to the shop. I bought apples, pears, and bananas.
Apostrophe 'Possession (dog's bone) or contraction (it's = it is)The children's toys. It's been a long day.
Semicolon ;Joins two related complete sentencesThe sun was shining; the birds were singing.
Colon :Introduces a list, explanation, or quotationHe brought three things: a book, a pen, and a map.
Dash —Creates a strong break or emphasisHe was — there was no other word — terrified.
Quotation Marks ""Enclose direct speech or quotationsShe said, "I'll be there soon."

📖 Common Grammar Mistakes to Avoid

ErrorRuleCorrect / Incorrect
Its vs It'sIts = possessive. It's = it is / it has.✅ The dog wagged its tail. It's raining.
Your vs You'reYour = possessive. You're = you are.✅ Your book is here. You're welcome.
There/Their/They'reThere = place. Their = possession. They're = they are.✅ There it is. Their house. They're coming.
To/Too/TwoTo = direction. Too = also/excess. Two = number.✅ Go to the shop. Me too. Two apples.
Comma SpliceDon't join two sentences with only a comma.❌ It was raining, I stayed inside. ✅ It was raining, so I stayed inside.
Sentence FragmentEvery sentence needs a subject and verb.❌ A large, empty room. ✅ She entered a large, empty room.
Subject-Verb AgreementSingular subject = singular verb.✅ The group of students is waiting. ❌ The group of students are waiting.

📝 Sentence Types

Simple Sentence One independent clause. E.g. "The dog barked." Creates impact and clarity. Compound Sentence Two independent clauses joined by a conjunction (and, but, or, so). E.g. "The dog barked, and the cat ran away." Complex Sentence One independent clause + one or more dependent clauses. E.g. "Although the dog barked loudly, the cat remained calm." Adds detail and sophistication.
🧠 Mnemonic: Its vs It's

Its = possession • It's = contraction
"The its with no apostrophe is the possessive one (like his/hers). The it's with an apostrophe is short for 'it is' or 'it has'. If you can replace it with 'it is', use the apostrophe."

🎯 Exam tip: SPaG marks are awarded in every written answer. Use a variety of punctuation (commas, full stops, apostrophes, semicolons) and sentence types (simple, compound, complex). Proofread your work before submitting — even one extra minute of checking can save marks.
