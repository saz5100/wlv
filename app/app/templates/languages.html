✍️ Academic Writing Guide

✍️ Academic Writing at University

Strong academic writing is essential for success at university. This guide covers the key skills you need: structuring essays, using Harvard referencing, maintaining an academic tone, building paragraphs with PEEL/TEEL, and avoiding common pitfalls. Whether you are writing your first assignment or polishing your final dissertation, these principles will help you communicate your ideas clearly and professionally.

📋 Essay Structure ▼

A well-structured essay guides the reader through your argument logically. Most academic essays follow a three-part structure:

📌

Introduction

Sets the scene: introduces the topic, provides context, states your thesis or argument, and outlines the structure of the essay. Typically 10–15% of the word count.

📄

Body Paragraphs

Develops your argument using PEEL/TEEL paragraphs. Each paragraph should focus on one main point, supported by evidence and analysis. Typically 70–80% of the word count.

🎯

Conclusion

Summarises your key arguments, restates your thesis in light of the evidence presented, and offers a final thought or implication. No new information. Typically 10–15% of the word count.

The Introduction — A Closer Look

A strong introduction should answer three questions for the reader:

  1. What is the topic? — Provide background context and define key terms.
  2. Why does it matter? — Explain the significance or debate around the topic.
  3. How will you address it? — State your thesis and signpost the essay structure.
✅ Example Introduction:
"The concept of sustainable development has become a central concern for governments worldwide. Whilst economic growth remains a priority, there is growing recognition that environmental degradation poses long-term risks to prosperity. This essay will argue that sustainable development is not merely an environmental ideal but an economic necessity, examining the evidence from three key sectors: energy, agriculture, and urban planning."

The Conclusion — A Closer Look

An effective conclusion should:

  • Restate your thesis in a fresh way (do not copy the introduction verbatim).
  • Summarise the main points from each body paragraph.
  • Offer a final reflection, implication, or recommendation.
  • Never introduce new evidence or arguments.
💡 Tip: Write your introduction after you have written the body paragraphs. You will have a clearer sense of your argument and can signpost more accurately.
🧱 PEEL / TEEL Paragraph Structure ▼

Every body paragraph should follow a clear structure. Two common frameworks are PEEL and TEEL. Both ensure your paragraphs are focused, evidence-based, and analytical.

P
Point

The topic sentence — state the main point of this paragraph in one clear sentence.

E
Evidence

Support your point with evidence: academic sources, data, case studies, or quotations.

E / A
Explanation / Analysis

Explain how the evidence supports your point. Analyse its significance — do not just describe it.

L
Link

Link back to the essay question or forward to the next paragraph to maintain flow.

TEEL is identical except the 'T' stands for Topic sentence (the same as 'Point' in PEEL). Use whichever framework your tutor prefers.

✅ Example PEEL Paragraph:
Point: The introduction of the National Curriculum in 1988 significantly altered the landscape of English education.
Evidence: As Baker (1989) argued, the Act established a standardised framework for what children should be taught, replacing the previous system of local authority discretion.
Explanation: This centralisation of control represented a fundamental shift from a teacher-led to a government-directed model of education, raising important questions about professional autonomy and the politicisation of knowledge.
Link: Having established the structural changes brought about by the 1988 Act, the following section will examine its impact on classroom practice.
💡 Tip: The 'Explanation' step is where most students lose marks. Ask yourself: "So what? Why does this evidence matter?" If you cannot answer that question, you need to analyse more deeply.
🎯 Academic Tone and Style ▼

Academic writing requires a formal, objective, and precise tone. The table below contrasts academic style with informal writing.

Feature Academic ✅ Informal ❌
Contractions Write in full: do not, cannot, it is, will not Avoid don't, can't, it's, won't
Personal pronouns Use passive or third-person: it can be argued, the evidence suggests Avoid I think, in my opinion, we believe
Vocabulary Precise, subject-specific terminology Avoid vague words: nice, good, bad, things, stuff
Hedging language Use suggests, indicates, may, could, potentially Avoid absolute claims: proves, always, never, definitely
Rhetorical questions Rarely appropriate — rephrase as a statement Avoid How can we solve this problem?
Colloquialisms Formal register throughout Avoid a lot of, loads of, basically, sort of
Emotive language Objective, evidence-based reasoning Avoid outrageous, terrible, wonderful, shocking

Hedging Language

Academic writing is cautious and precise. Use hedging language to express claims with appropriate certainty:

Too Absolute ❌ Better (Hedged) ✅
This proves that...This suggests that...
Smith's research shows...Smith's research indicates...
This is the only explanation...This may be one explanation...
It is obvious that...It could be argued that...
This always happens when...This often occurs when...
💡 Tip: Read your work aloud. If it sounds like something you would say in a conversation, it is probably too informal. Academic writing should sound considered and precise.
📚 Harvard Referencing ▼

Harvard referencing is the standard citation style at the University of Wolverhampton. It uses an author-date system: you cite the author's surname and year of publication in the text, and provide full details in a reference list at the end.

In-Text Citations

Type Example
Direct quote As Smith (2019, p. 45) argues, "education is a fundamental human right."
Paraphrase Education has been described as a fundamental human right (Smith, 2019).
Two authors Recent research supports this view (Jones and Patel, 2020).
Three+ authors This approach has been widely adopted (Williams et al., 2021).
Multiple sources Several studies confirm this finding (Brown, 2018; Davies, 2020; Khan, 2022).
No author Use the title in italics: (Education Policy Review, 2020).
Organisation as author The World Health Organization (WHO, 2021) reported that...

Reference List Format

The reference list appears at the end of your essay, arranged alphabetically by author surname. Key formats:

📖 Book
Author, S. (Year) Title in Italics. Edition. Place: Publisher.

Smith, J. (2019) Understanding Education Policy. 2nd edn. London: Routledge.
📰 Journal Article
Author, S. (Year) 'Article title', Journal Name, Volume(Issue), pp. pages.

Jones, A. and Patel, R. (2020) 'Inclusive pedagogy in higher education', Journal of Education Studies, 45(3), pp. 210–228.
🌐 Website
Author, S. (Year) Page Title. Available at: URL (Accessed: date).

Department for Education (2023) National Curriculum Overview. Available at: https://www.gov.uk/national-curriculum (Accessed: 15 January 2024).
📄 Chapter in Edited Book
Author, S. (Year) 'Chapter title', in Editor, E. (ed.) Book Title. Place: Publisher, pp. pages.

Khan, M. (2022) 'Assessment and accountability', in Brown, T. (ed.) Contemporary Issues in Education. London: Palgrave, pp. 88–104.
⚠️ Common Referencing Mistakes:
  • Forgetting to include page numbers for direct quotations.
  • Mixing up the order of author names and publication year.
  • Including sources in the reference list that are not cited in the text (and vice versa).
  • Using inconsistent formatting — check your university's style guide.
💡 Tip: Use a reference manager such as Zotero, Mendeley, or EndNote from the start. They save hours of formatting and reduce errors. The University of Wolverhampton Library provides free access to these tools.
⚠️ Common Writing Pitfalls ▼

Even strong writers fall into these traps. Recognising them is the first step to avoiding them.

🚫 1. Descriptive Writing Instead of Critical Analysis

Simply describing what happened or what an author said is not enough. You must evaluate, compare, and question. "Smith argues X, but Jones counters that Y" is stronger than "Smith argues X."

✅ Fix: Ask "So what?" after every descriptive sentence.
📝 2. Overly Long or Rambling Sentences

Sentences that run on for several lines lose the reader. If a sentence has more than two clauses, consider splitting it. Aim for clarity over complexity.

✅ Fix: Read each sentence aloud. If you run out of breath, split it.
🔁 3. Repetition and Wordiness

Using the same words or phrases repeatedly weakens your writing. Similarly, padding sentences with unnecessary words (e.g. "in order to" → "to"; "due to the fact that" → "because") makes your work less concise.

✅ Fix: Use a thesaurus sparingly and cut every word that does not add meaning.
📖 4. Over-Quoting

Quotations should support your argument, not replace it. A string of block quotes with little analysis suggests you have not engaged with the material. Paraphrase where possible and always explain why a quote matters.

✅ Fix: For every quote, write at least two sentences of your own analysis.
🔗 5. Weak or Missing Topic Sentences

Every paragraph should begin with a clear topic sentence that states the main point. Without it, the reader has to guess what the paragraph is about.

✅ Fix: The first sentence of each paragraph should answer: "What is this paragraph about?"
📉 6. Lack of Signposting and Transitions

Essays that jump abruptly from one idea to the next confuse the reader. Use transition words and phrases to guide the reader through your argument.

✅ Fix: Use signposting: however, furthermore, in contrast, consequently, nevertheless, on the other hand.
📚 7. Poor Referencing and Accidental Plagiarism

Failing to cite sources properly — even unintentionally — is a serious academic offence. Keep careful notes of where you found each idea, and cite as you write, not at the end.

✅ Fix: Record full citation details the moment you find a source. Never copy-paste without immediately adding the reference.
🎯 8. Going Off-Topic

It is easy to include interesting but irrelevant material. Every paragraph should directly support your thesis or answer the essay question. If it does not, cut it.

✅ Fix: Keep the essay question pinned above your workspace. Check each paragraph against it.
💡 Tip: After finishing your first draft, step away for a few hours (or overnight). Return to it with fresh eyes — you will spot weaknesses you missed while writing.
✅ Proofreading and Editing Checklist ▼

Use this checklist before submitting any assignment:

✓ Check
☐Structure: Does the essay have a clear introduction, body, and conclusion?
☐Thesis: Is the main argument clearly stated in the introduction?
☐Paragraphs: Does each paragraph follow PEEL/TEEL structure?
☐Topic sentences: Does every paragraph begin with a clear topic sentence?
☐Evidence: Is every claim supported by a credible academic source?
☐Analysis: Have you explained why the evidence matters, not just described it?
☐Referencing: Are all in-text citations correctly formatted in Harvard style?
☐Reference list: Does every in-text citation appear in the reference list (and vice versa)?
☐Tone: Is the language formal, objective, and free of contractions?
☐Hedging: Are claims expressed with appropriate caution?
☐Spelling: Have you used UK English spelling (e.g. colour, organise, centre, behaviour)?
☐Grammar: Are sentences complete? No comma splices or sentence fragments?
☐Word count: Does the essay meet the required word count (or fall within the acceptable range)?
☐Transitions: Are there clear links between paragraphs and sections?
☐Title page: Does it include your name, module title, tutor name, and submission date?
💡 Tip: Print your essay and proofread on paper — errors are much easier to spot on a physical page than on a screen. Alternatively, change the font or read it backwards (sentence by sentence) to break the flow and catch mistakes.

📖 Key Terminology for Academic Writing

These are the essential terms you need to know for university-level writing.

Thesis
The central argument or claim of your essay. Every paragraph should support your thesis.
Topic Sentence
The first sentence of a paragraph that states its main point. Guides the reader through your argument.
Signposting
Words and phrases that guide the reader through your essay (e.g. however, furthermore, in contrast).
Hedging
Using cautious language (suggests, may, could) to express claims with appropriate certainty rather than overstating them.
Paraphrasing
Restating someone else's ideas in your own words while still citing the original source. Preferred over direct quotations.
Citation
An in-text reference to a source, using the author-date format in Harvard style (e.g. Smith, 2019).
Reference List
A complete list of all sources cited in your essay, arranged alphabetically by author surname at the end of the document.
Critical Analysis
Evaluating and questioning evidence rather than simply describing it. Involves comparing sources, identifying strengths and weaknesses, and forming your own judgement.
Plagiarism
Presenting someone else's work or ideas as your own without proper attribution. A serious academic offence that can result in failure or expulsion.
Register
The level of formality in language. Academic writing requires a formal register: no contractions, slang, or colloquial expressions.
Cohesion
The logical flow and connection between sentences and paragraphs. Achieved through signposting, transitions, and consistent referencing of the main argument.
Abstract
A concise summary of a research article, thesis, or essay. Usually 150–250 words, covering the purpose, methods, key findings, and conclusions.
