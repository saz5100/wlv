✍️ SPaG and Writing Guide

✍️ SPaG — Spelling, Punctuation and Grammar

SPaG stands for Spelling, Punctuation and Grammar. In the WLV English Equivalency exam, SPaG is assessed as part of Question 5 (creative or descriptive writing), where it contributes to your overall marks. Strong SPaG makes your writing clear, credible, and easy to read. This guide covers the essential rules, strategies, and techniques you need to maximise your SPaG marks.

🔤 Punctuation Rules ▼

Punctuation gives structure to your writing. Using a range of punctuation accurately signals control of written English and improves the clarity of your ideas.

Mark Rule Example
. Full stop Ends a sentence. Use one sentence per complete idea. The rain fell steadily against the windowpane.
, Comma Separates items in a list, clauses, or introductory phrases. Do not join two complete sentences with only a comma (comma splice). She walked slowly, carefully, and deliberately across the room.
Avoid: She walked slowly, she was tired.
? Question mark Ends a direct question. Do not use a full stop after a question. What could he possibly be thinking?
! Exclamation mark Shows strong emotion or emphasis. Use sparingly — one per paragraph at most. Overuse weakens the effect. \"Stop!\" she cried, her voice trembling.
: Colon Introduces a list, a quotation, or an explanation. What follows the colon explains or expands on what came before. He had only one goal: survival.
; Semicolon Links two closely related independent clauses without a conjunction. Also separates complex list items. The sky was dark; the storm had arrived.
' Apostrophe Possession: shows ownership. Singular = 's; plural ending in s = s'.
Contraction: shows omitted letters. It's = it is; its = belonging to it.		 The student's book (one student)
The students' books (many students)
Common error: it's vs its
\" Speech marks Enclose direct speech or quotations. Punctuation goes inside the closing quotation mark in UK English. \"I can't believe it,\" she whispered.
— Dash Creates a strong break or interruption in a sentence. More dramatic than a comma. Use sparingly for effect. He was — there was no other word for it — terrified.
( ) Brackets Enclose additional information that is not essential to the main sentence. The sentence should still make sense without the bracketed content. The results (see appendix A) confirmed the hypothesis.

Punctuation for Effect

In creative writing, punctuation can create pace and mood:

Effect Technique
Fast pace Short sentences separated by full stops. He ran. He stumbled. He fell.
Slow pace Longer sentences with commas, colons, and semicolons. The mist crept slowly, silently, inexorably across the moor.
Tension Dashes for abrupt interruptions. She turned the key — the door creaked — something was wrong.
Clarity Colons to introduce explanations. The reason was simple: he had never been taught.
💡 Tip: In the WLV exam, aim to use at least four different punctuation marks in your Question 5 response. A mix of full stops, commas, colons/semicolons, and dashes or speech marks shows the examiner you have control.
📐 Grammar Rules ▼

Grammar is the system that governs how words combine to form sentences. Getting the basics right is essential for clear communication and higher SPaG marks.

Subject–Verb Agreement

The subject and verb must agree in number (singular or plural).

Correct ✅ Incorrect ❌
The dog runs (singular subject + singular verb)The dog run
The dogs run (plural subject + plural verb)The dogs runs
Everyone is here (indefinite pronoun = singular)Everyone are here
Neither of the answers is correctNeither of the answers are correct

Sentence Types

Type Definition Example
Simple One independent clause. One main idea. The sun set over the hills.
Compound Two independent clauses joined by a conjunction (and, but, or, so). The sun set, and the stars appeared.
Complex One independent clause + one or more subordinate clauses. Although the sun had set, the sky remained bright.

Tense Consistency

Once you establish a tense, stick with it unless there is a clear reason to shift. Shifting tenses mid-paragraph confuses the reader.

Consistent ✅ Inconsistent ❌
She walked to the door and opened it. The room was empty. (past tense)She walked to the door and opens it. The room is empty.
He runs every morning and feels energised. (present tense)He runs every morning and felt energised.

Common Grammar Traps

Trap Explanation Example
Comma splice Joining two complete sentences with only a comma. Wrong: It was raining, we stayed inside.
Fix: It was raining, so we stayed inside.
Run-on sentence Two or more sentences joined without any punctuation. Wrong: She arrived late the train had already left.
Fix: She arrived late. The train had already left.
Sentence fragment An incomplete sentence that lacks a main verb or subject. Wrong: Although she was tired.
Fix: Although she was tired, she continued working.
Misplaced modifier A word or phrase placed too far from the word it modifies. Wrong: Walking down the street, the house looked abandoned.
Fix: Walking down the street, she noticed the house looked abandoned.
💡 Tip: Vary your sentence types in Question 5. A paragraph of only simple sentences sounds choppy; a paragraph of only complex sentences sounds dense. Mix short, medium, and long sentences for rhythm and impact.
📝 Spelling Strategies ▼

Spelling errors can cost you marks even when your ideas are strong. Use these strategies to improve your spelling and avoid common mistakes.

UK English Spelling

The WLV exam uses UK English spelling conventions. These differ from US English in several key patterns:

Pattern UK English ✅ US English (avoid)
-our / -or colour, honour, behaviour, flavour, neighbour color, honor, behavior, flavor, neighbor
-re / -er centre, theatre, metre, litre center, theater, meter, liter
-ise / -ize organise, realise, recognise, emphasise organize, realize, recognize, emphasize
-yse / -yze analyse, catalyse, paralyse analyze, catalyze, paralyze
-ence / -ense defence, licence, offence, pretence defense, license, offense, pretense
-ll- / -l- travelled, labelled, cancelled, marvellous traveled, labeled, canceled, marvelous
-ogue / -og catalogue, dialogue, monologue, analogue catalog, dialog, monolog, analog

Commonly Misspelled Words

Correct ✅ Common Mistake ❌
accommodation (double c, double m)accomodation, acommodation
embarrassed (double r, double s)embarassed, embarrassed
necessary (one c, double s)neccessary, neccesary
separate (a in the middle, not e)seperate
definitely (finite + ly)definately, definately
occurrence (double c, double r)occurance, occurence
privilege (i before lege)privelege, priviledge
rhythm (h after r, no vowels in second syllable)rythm, rythym
conscious (science + ous)concious, consious
government (n before m)goverment, goverment

Spelling Strategies

  1. Look, Say, Cover, Write, Check: Look at the word, say it aloud, cover it, write it from memory, then check. Repeat until correct.
  2. Break it down: Split long words into syllables. Un-for-tun-ate-ly is easier to spell than unfortunately.
  3. Use mnemonics: Create memorable phrases. Big Elephants Can't Always Use Small Exits = because.
  4. Know the rules: i before e except after c — but watch for exceptions (weird, science, sufficient).
  5. Keep a personal spelling log: Write down words you commonly misspell and review them regularly.
  6. Proofread specifically for spelling: Read your work backwards (last word to first) to focus on individual words rather than meaning.
💡 Tip: In the exam, if you are unsure of a spelling, try a different word that means the same thing. It is better to use a simpler word correctly than a complex word incorrectly.
🎨 Writing Techniques — Imagery, Tone and Vocabulary ▼

Question 5 in the WLV exam tests your ability to write creatively and descriptively. Using a range of writing techniques will help you achieve higher marks for content, organisation, and SPaG.

Imagery and Figurative Language

Technique Definition Example
Simile Comparing two things using like or as. The fog hung over the city like a grey blanket.
Metaphor Describing one thing as if it were another (direct comparison). The classroom was a prison, its walls closing in on him.
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things. The wind whispered through the trees.
Pathetic fallacy Using weather to reflect mood or emotion. The storm raged as his anger boiled over.
Sensory imagery Describing what can be seen, heard, smelt, tasted, or touched. The sharp tang of salt hung in the air; the crash of waves echoed in the distance.
Onomatopoeia Words that sound like the noise they describe. The branches snapped and crackled underfoot.
Alliteration Repeating the same initial sound in nearby words. The silent, silver snow settled on the street.

Tone and Atmosphere

Tone is the overall mood or feeling of your writing. Choose vocabulary and sentence structures that match the tone you want to create.

Tone Vocabulary and Techniques Example
Sinister / Menacing Dark, shadowy, lurking, ominous, creeping, suffocating. Short sentences. Personification of darkness. Shadows crept along the walls. The silence was suffocating. Something was watching.
Joyful / Peaceful Warm, golden, gentle, soft, glowing, serene. Sensory imagery of light and warmth. Longer, flowing sentences. Golden sunlight filtered through the leaves, dappling the grass with warmth.
Chaotic / Urgent Frantic, pounding, crashing, screaming, racing. Short, sharp sentences. Onomatopoeia. Exclamation marks. Bang! The door flew open. Chaos erupted. People screamed and scattered.
Melancholy / Reflective Fading, grey, distant, hollow, forgotten, weary. Slow pace. Semicolons and colons for thoughtful pauses. The house stood empty; its windows like hollow eyes staring at nothing.

Vocabulary — Choosing the Right Words

Precise vocabulary makes your writing vivid and engaging. Replace weak, vague words with stronger, more specific alternatives.

bigenormous, vast, colossal, immenseDescribing size
smallminute, tiny, minuscule, compactDescribing size

saidwhispered, shouted, murmured, exclaimed, mutteredDescribing speech

✅ Before and After — Vocabulary Upgrade:
Weak: The big house looked old and scary. A bad smell came from inside. A man walked to the door and said something.
Strong: The vast mansion loomed, its crumbling facade shrouded in shadow. A foul, musty stench seeped from within. A figure crept towards the door and muttered a single, chilling word.
💡 Tip: Do not try to use every technique in one paragraph. Choose two or three techniques and use them well. A single well-crafted metaphor is worth more than a paragraph full of forced similes.
⚠️ Common SPaG Mistakes ▼

These are the most frequent SPaG errors that cost students marks in the WLV exam. Learn to spot and fix them.

🔤 1. Its vs It's

Its = possessive (belonging to it). It's = contraction of it is or it has. If you can replace it with it is, use an apostrophe. If not, do not.

✅ Fix: The dog wagged its tail. / It's raining outside.
📝 2. There / Their / They're

There = a place or existence. Their = belonging to them. They're = they are.

✅ Fix: They're putting their coats over there.
📐 3. Your / You're

Your = belonging to you. You're = you are.

✅ Fix: You're going to love your new course.
🔗 4. To / Too / Two

To = direction or infinitive. Too = also or excessively. Two = the number 2.

✅ Fix: I need to buy two tickets too.
📖 5. Then vs Than

Then = next in time or sequence. Than = used for comparisons.

✅ Fix: First we studied, then we ate. She is taller than him.
🎯 6. Affect vs Effect

Affect = verb (to influence). Effect = noun (the result).

✅ Fix: The weather affected the crops. The effect was devastating.
✂️ 7. Comma Splices

Using a comma to join two complete sentences without a conjunction. This is one of the most common grammar errors in student writing.

✅ Fix: Use a full stop, a semicolon, or add a conjunction (and, but, so, because).
🔁 8. Apostrophes in Plurals

Do not use an apostrophe to form a plural. Apostrophes are for possession and contractions only.

✅ Fix: Apple's 50p ❌ → Apples 50p ✅. The 1990's ❌ → The 1990s ✅.
📏 9. Double Negatives

Using two negatives in the same clause creates a positive (or confusion). Avoid them in formal writing.

✅ Fix: I don't have nothing ❌ → I have nothing ✅ or I don't have anything ✅.
📚 10. Confusing Homophones

Words that sound the same but have different spellings and meanings are easy to mix up under exam pressure.

✅ Watch for: accept/except, advice/advise, practice/practise, licence/license, stationary/stationery, principal/principle, weather/whether, whose/who's.
💡 Tip: Create a personal checklist of the three SPaG errors you make most often. In the exam, save the last two minutes to check specifically for those errors.
🎯 Exam Tips for SPaG and Writing ▼

These strategies will help you maximise your SPaG marks in the WLV English Equivalency exam, particularly in Question 5 (creative/descriptive writing).

Before You Write — Planning (5 minutes)

  1. Read the prompt carefully: Identify whether it is a descriptive (describe a scene) or narrative (tell a story) task. Each requires a different approach.
  2. Brainstorm vocabulary: Jot down 5–10 ambitious words and phrases you want to use. Think about sensory details, figurative language, and precise verbs.
  3. Plan your structure: Opening (set the scene) → Build (develop the atmosphere or action) → Ending (resolution or final image). A clear structure helps the examiner follow your writing.
  4. Decide on your tone: Will it be sinister, joyful, tense, or reflective? Choose vocabulary and sentence structures that match.

While You Write (30–35 minutes)

✓ Check
☐Start strong: Your opening sentence should hook the reader. Use imagery, a striking detail, or an unusual perspective.
☐Show, don't tell: Instead of "He was scared", describe what fear looks like: "His hands trembled; cold sweat beaded on his forehead."
☐Vary sentence length: Mix short, punchy sentences with longer, flowing ones to create rhythm.
☐Use a range of punctuation: Aim for at least four different punctuation marks (full stops, commas, colons, dashes, semicolons, speech marks).
☐Stay in one tense: If you start in the past tense, stay in the past tense. Only shift tense for a deliberate effect (e.g. a flashback).
☐Keep paragraphs focused: Start a new paragraph when you move to a new idea, time, or place. Each paragraph should have one main focus.
☐Use dialogue sparingly: If you include speech, punctuate it correctly with speech marks and new lines for each speaker.

After You Write — Proofreading (5 minutes)

  1. Read your work aloud — you will hear errors that your eyes skip over.
  2. Check for capital letters: Start of sentences, proper nouns (names, places, titles), and the pronoun I.
  3. Check apostrophes: Are they in the right place? Is it its or it's? Is it student's or students'?
  4. Check for full stops: Does every sentence end with a full stop, question mark, or exclamation mark? No run-on sentences?
  5. Check UK spelling: colour not color, organise not organize, centre not center.
  6. Check your personal error list: Look specifically for the three mistakes you know you make most often.

Quick Reference — SPaG Mark Scheme

In the WLV exam, SPaG is marked across three levels. Understanding what the examiner is looking for helps you target your efforts.

Level What the Examiner Sees How to Achieve It
High Consistently accurate spelling and punctuation. Wide range of punctuation used effectively. Grammatical control throughout. Precise, ambitious vocabulary. Use 4+ punctuation marks. Vary sentence types. Use figurative language. No spelling errors. UK English throughout.
Middle Generally accurate spelling and punctuation. Some range of punctuation. Occasional grammatical errors that do not impede meaning. Some ambitious vocabulary. Use 2–3 punctuation marks. Mostly correct sentences. A few spelling errors but not in key words. Some attempt at ambitious vocabulary.
Low Frequent spelling and punctuation errors. Limited range of punctuation. Grammatical errors that sometimes impede meaning. Simple vocabulary. Focus on getting the basics right: full stops, capital letters, simple sentences. Use words you can spell confidently.
✅ Model Opening — High Level:
The market square was a symphony of chaos. Stalls overflowed with vibrant produce — crimson tomatoes, emerald peppers, golden lemons piled high. The air hummed with the chatter of vendors, the clatter of crates, and the rich, earthy aroma of fresh bread. An old woman, her face weathered like ancient leather, beckoned me closer with a gnarled finger. \"Taste this,\" she whispered, pressing a sun-warmed fig into my palm.
💡 Key Advice: The WLV exam rewards quality over quantity. A well-crafted, carefully proofread response of two sides will score higher than a rushed, error-filled response of three sides. Take the time to plan, write with care, and proofread thoroughly.

📖 Key SPaG Terminology

Essential terms you need to know for the WLV English Equivalency exam.

Noun
A naming word for a person, place, thing, or idea (e.g. student, city, courage, book).
Verb
A doing or being word (e.g. run, exist, think, become). Every sentence needs at least one.
Adjective
A word that describes a noun (e.g. dark, enormous, fragile, silent).
Adverb
A word that describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb (e.g. slowly, very, carefully, almost).
Conjunction
A word that joins clauses or sentences (e.g. and, but, because, although, so).
Preposition
A word that shows position, time, or direction (e.g. in, on, at, under, between, through).
Clause
A group of words containing a subject and a verb. An independent clause can stand alone as a sentence.
Homophone
Words that sound the same but have different spellings and meanings (e.g. there/their/they're, to/too/two).
Register
The level of formality in language. Exam writing requires a formal or semi-formal register.
Tense
The time of the action or state of being — past, present, or future. Keep tense consistent within your writing.
Figurative Language
Language that uses comparison or association for effect — similes, metaphors, personification, and symbolism.
Syntax
The arrangement of words and phrases to create well-formed sentences. Good syntax makes your meaning clear.
