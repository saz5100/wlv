🔬 Research Methods

Qualitative and quantitative approaches, data collection, ethics, and dissertation planning — essential for your WLV research project

🔬 Research Methods — WLV

This guide covers the core research methods you will need for your WLV research project and dissertation. Whether you are conducting a small-scale study or a full undergraduate dissertation, understanding the distinction between qualitative and quantitative approaches, selecting appropriate data collection techniques, navigating research ethics, and planning your dissertation structure are all essential skills.

📑 Sections

📊 1. Qualitative vs Quantitative Research

Research methods in the social sciences and computing disciplines generally fall into two broad categories: qualitative and quantitative. Many WLV research projects adopt a mixed-methods approach, combining elements of both.

Dimension Quantitative Qualitative
Purpose Measure, test hypotheses, identify patterns and relationships Explore, understand, interpret meanings and experiences
Data Type Numerical data — counts, scores, percentages, statistics Textual or visual data — interviews, observations, documents
Sample Size Larger samples (often 30+) for statistical power Smaller samples (5–30) for depth and richness
Approach Deductive — theory first, then data collection to test it Inductive — data first, then develop themes and theory
Analysis Statistical tests (t-tests, ANOVA, regression, chi-square) Thematic analysis, content analysis, grounded theory
Generalisability High — findings can be applied to wider populations Low — findings are context-specific and transferable
Common Methods Surveys, experiments, structured observations, secondary data Interviews, focus groups, case studies, ethnography

Mixed Methods Combining both approaches can provide a more complete picture. For example, a survey (quantitative) might identify a trend, while follow-up interviews (qualitative) explain why that trend exists.

📋 2. Data Collection Techniques

Choosing the right data collection method depends on your research question, the type of data you need, and the practical constraints of your project (time, access, resources).

📝 Surveys and Questionnaires

  • Efficient for collecting data from large numbers of participants
  • Can include closed questions (quantitative) and open-ended questions (qualitative)
  • Online tools (e.g. Microsoft Forms, Google Forms, JISC Online Surveys) make distribution and analysis straightforward
  • Pilot your survey to identify ambiguous or leading questions before full deployment

🎙️ Interviews

  • Structured: Fixed questions, same order for all participants — easier to compare
  • Semi-structured: Key questions with flexibility to probe interesting responses — most common in WLV dissertations
  • Unstructured: Open conversation guided by the participant — rich data but harder to analyse
  • Record and transcribe interviews for accurate analysis; obtain consent beforehand

👥 Focus Groups

  • Small group discussion (typically 6–10 participants) facilitated by the researcher
  • Useful for exploring shared experiences, attitudes, and group dynamics
  • Participants may build on each other's ideas, generating richer data than individual interviews
  • Requires careful moderation to ensure all voices are heard

🔬 Experiments

  • Manipulate an independent variable to observe its effect on a dependent variable
  • Control groups and random assignment help establish causality
  • Common in computing, psychology, and health-related research
  • Laboratory experiments offer high control; field experiments offer greater realism

📄 Secondary Data Analysis

  • Analysing existing datasets (government statistics, organisational records, open data)
  • Time-efficient and often free from ethical approval delays
  • Check the data's original purpose, quality, and limitations before use

📚 Document and Content Analysis

  • Systematic analysis of texts, policies, reports, social media, or other documents
  • Can be quantitative (counting frequencies of words/themes) or qualitative (interpreting meaning)
  • Useful for historical research, policy analysis, and media studies
⚖️ 3. Research Ethics

All research involving human participants at WLV must be conducted ethically. You will need to submit an ethics application to your supervisor or the university ethics committee before starting data collection.

Core Ethical Principles

  • Informed Consent: Participants must understand what the research involves and voluntarily agree to take part. Provide a participant information sheet and a consent form.
  • Confidentiality and Anonymity: Protect participants' identities. Use pseudonyms, store data securely, and do not share identifiable information.
  • Right to Withdraw: Participants can leave the study at any time without penalty. Make this clear at the outset.
  • Avoiding Harm: Ensure no physical, psychological, or emotional harm comes to participants. Sensitive topics require additional care.
  • Transparency: Be honest about the purpose of your research. Deception should only be used when absolutely necessary and must be debriefed afterwards.
  • Data Protection: Comply with UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. Store data securely and delete it after the required retention period.

WLV Ethics Checklist

  • Have you completed the WLV ethics self-assessment form?
  • Have you prepared a participant information sheet in plain English?
  • Have you designed a consent form with all required sections?
  • Have you considered how you will store and anonymise your data?
  • Have you discussed your ethics application with your supervisor?
  • Have you allowed enough time for ethics approval (2–4 weeks minimum)?
  • Have you considered the ethical implications of your data collection methods?
  • Have you planned how to debrief participants after the study?

Remember Ethical approval must be obtained before any data collection begins. Retrospective approval is not possible.

📅 4. Dissertation Planning

A well-structured dissertation is the culmination of your WLV degree. Planning early and breaking the process into manageable phases is key to success.

Typical Dissertation Structure

📌 Introduction Research question, aims and objectives, rationale, scope, and structure of the dissertation
📚 Literature Review Critical review of existing research, identification of gaps, theoretical framework
🔧 Methodology Research design, data collection methods, sampling strategy, ethical considerations, limitations
📊 Findings / Results Presentation of data collected — tables, charts, themes, without interpretation
💬 Discussion Interpretation of findings, connection to literature, implications, unexpected results
✅ Conclusion Summary of key findings, contribution to knowledge, limitations, recommendations for future research

Planning Timeline (Suggested)

Phase Weeks Key Activities
Topic Selection 1–2 Identify broad area, read around the topic, refine research question
Proposal 2–3 Write research proposal, get supervisor approval, submit ethics application
Literature Review 3–6 Systematic search, critical reading, synthesise sources, identify gaps
Methodology 6–8 Design study, pilot instruments, recruit participants, finalise ethics
Data Collection 8–12 Gather data, monitor progress, handle issues as they arise
Data Analysis 12–15 Transcribe, code, run statistical tests, identify themes
Writing Up 15–20 Draft chapters, revise, get feedback from supervisor
Final Submission 20–22 Proofread, format, check referencing, submit

Top Tip Start writing early — even rough notes and bullet points can be refined later. Leave at least two weeks for proofreading and formatting before the submission deadline.

📖 5. Literature Reviews

A literature review is a critical evaluation of existing research on your topic. It is not simply a summary of what others have written — you must synthesise, compare, and critique the literature to establish the context for your own research.

Purpose of a Literature Review

  • Demonstrate your knowledge of the field and its key debates
  • Identify gaps, contradictions, or unanswered questions that justify your research
  • Position your work within the existing body of knowledge
  • Identify appropriate theoretical frameworks and methodologies
  • Avoid reinventing the wheel — build on what is already known

Steps for a Systematic Literature Review

  1. Define your research question — be specific and focused
  2. Identify databases and search terms — use WLV Library Search, Google Scholar, IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, Scopus, PubMed, etc.
  3. Set inclusion and exclusion criteria — publication date, peer-reviewed only, specific methodology, etc.
  4. Conduct the search — record your search terms and the number of results for transparency
  5. Screen results — review titles and abstracts, then full texts against your criteria
  6. Extract key information — author, year, methodology, key findings, limitations
  7. Synthesise and critique — group studies by theme, compare findings, identify strengths and weaknesses
  8. Write the review — organise thematically, not chronologically, and always link back to your research question

Critical Reading Questions

When reading a source, ask yourself:

  • What is the author's main argument or claim?
  • What evidence do they provide to support it?
  • Is the methodology appropriate and rigorous?
  • Are there any biases or limitations in the study?
  • How does this source relate to other studies in the field?
  • Does it support, contradict, or extend existing knowledge?
  • How relevant is it to my own research question?

Referencing and Citation Management

  • Use a consistent referencing style (WLV typically uses Harvard (Cite Them Right) or APA 7th)
  • Use reference management software such as Zotero, Mendeley, or EndNote to organise your sources
  • Keep a running bibliography from day one — retroactively adding references is time-consuming and error-prone
  • Check WLV's referencing guide for specific requirements in your department

Remember A good literature review is critical, not descriptive. Do not simply list what each author said — compare, contrast, and evaluate.
