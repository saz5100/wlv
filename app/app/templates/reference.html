This page brings together key frameworks, referencing guides, and reference information for your university studies. Use it as a quick lookup during assignments or revision.

📝 Harvard Referencing Guide

Key Rules

  • List authors by surname, then initials
  • Use "and" between authors, not "&"
  • Italicise book titles and journal names
  • Use single quotation marks for article/chapter titles
  • Include the date you accessed online sources
📊 Essay Structure Framework

Introduction (10-15% of word count)

  • Hook: Engage the reader with a relevant opening
  • Context: Provide background information on the topic
  • Thesis statement: Your main argument or position
  • Roadmap: Outline the structure of your essay

Body Paragraphs — PEEL Structure

  • Point — Make a clear claim or argument
  • Evidence — Support with academic sources, data, or examples
  • Explanation — Explain how the evidence supports your point
  • Link — Connect back to the thesis or to the next paragraph

Conclusion (10-15% of word count)

  • Synthesis: Summarise key arguments without introducing new information
  • Implications: Discuss the significance of your findings
  • Limitations: Acknowledge any limitations of your analysis
  • Future research: Suggest areas for further investigation
🔍 Critical Thinking Frameworks

CRAAP Test — Evaluating Sources

  • Currency — When was it published? Is it up to date?
  • Relevance — Does it relate to your topic? Is it at the right level?
  • Authority — Who wrote it? What are their credentials?
  • Accuracy — Is the information reliable? Is it peer-reviewed?
  • Purpose — Why was it written? Is there bias?

Common Logical Fallacies

  • Ad hominem: Attacking the person instead of the argument
  • Straw man: Misrepresenting an argument to make it easier to attack
  • False dichotomy: Presenting only two options when more exist
  • Slippery slope: Arguing that one step will inevitably lead to extreme outcomes
  • Circular reasoning: Using the conclusion as evidence for itself
  • Appeal to authority: Using an authority figure as evidence without supporting reasoning

🔄 Reflective Practice Models

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988)

  1. Description: What happened?
  2. Feelings: What were you thinking and feeling?
  3. Evaluation: What was good and bad about the experience?
  4. Analysis: What sense can you make of the situation?
  5. Conclusion: What else could you have done?
  6. Action Plan: What will you do next time?

Kolb's Learning Cycle (1984)

  1. Concrete Experience: Doing something
  2. Reflective Observation: Reviewing what happened
  3. Abstract Conceptualisation: Learning from the experience
  4. Active Experimentation: Trying out what you learned
