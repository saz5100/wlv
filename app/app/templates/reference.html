📖 Reference

Quick-reference tables and key concept summaries for WLV English Equivalency. Use these to revise language techniques, structural devices, and SPaG rules — essential knowledge for the exam.

📝 Language Techniques ▼
Technique Definition Example Effect
Simile Comparison using 'like' or 'as' "The fog crept like a silent grey blanket" Creates a vivid image; helps reader visualise
Metaphor Direct comparison stating one thing IS another "The sunset was a masterpiece of colour" Creates a powerful, direct image; suggests deeper meaning
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things "The wind howled with icy fingers" Makes the abstract feel alive and threatening
Imagery Language that appeals to the senses "The air was thick with the smell of spices" Creates a vivid sensory experience for the reader
Alliteration Repetition of initial consonant sounds "The sly, slithering snake" Creates rhythm; draws attention to key words
Onomatopoeia Words that sound like their meaning "The tap, tap, tap of rain" Creates auditory effect; makes description more vivid
Oxymoron Two contradictory terms placed together "Deafening silence" Creates surprise; highlights complexity or tension
Hyperbole Deliberate exaggeration for effect "I've told you a million times" Emphasises a point; creates humour or drama
Pathetic Fallacy Weather reflecting the mood/emotion "The storm raged as he wept" Creates atmosphere; mirrors character's emotions
Rhetorical Question Question that does not expect an answer "How could anyone forget?" Engages reader; makes them think; persuasive
Key Point: When analysing language, always state the technique, quote the example, and explain the effect on the reader. Use the formula: Technique + Quote + Effect.
🏗️ Structural Devices ▼
Device Definition Effect on Reader
Cyclical Structure Ending mirrors the beginning Creates a sense of completeness or inevitability; suggests nothing has changed
Flashback Shift to an earlier time Provides context or backstory; creates contrast between past and present
Foreshadowing Hints at future events Builds tension and anticipation; creates dramatic irony
Pace Speed of the narrative (fast or slow) Short sentences = fast pace (urgency, panic). Long sentences = slow pace (reflection, calm)
Sentence Length Variation Mixing short and long sentences Creates rhythm; short sentences create impact; long sentences build detail
Paragraphing Organisation of ideas into paragraphs Short paragraphs = emphasis, change of focus. Long paragraphs = detailed development
Zoom In/Out Moving from wide view to close detail (or vice versa) Creates cinematic effect; helps reader visualise the scene
Listing (Tricolon) Three items listed together Creates rhythm and emphasis; builds intensity
Juxtaposition Placing contrasting ideas close together Highlights differences; creates tension or surprise
Ellipsis Trailing off with ... Creates suspense; suggests something unsaid; slows pace
Key Point: Structure is about why the writer has organised the text in a particular way. Always link structural choices to the effect on the reader's experience.
📝 SPaG — Punctuation & Grammar ▼

🔤 Punctuation

Mark Usage Example
Full Stop . Ends a complete sentence The sun was shining.
Comma , Separates items in a list; after fronted adverbials; before conjunctions First, I went to the shop. I bought apples, pears, and bananas.
Apostrophe ' Possession (dog's bone) or contraction (it's = it is) The children's toys. It's been a long day.
Semicolon ; Joins two related complete sentences without a conjunction The sun was shining; the birds were singing.
Colon : Introduces a list, explanation, or quotation He brought three things: a book, a pen, and a map.
Dash — Creates a strong break or emphasis He was — there was no other word for it — terrified.
Quotation Marks "" Enclose direct speech or quotations She said, "I'll be there soon."

📖 Grammar Rules

Rule Explanation Correct / Incorrect
Subject-Verb Agreement Singular subject = singular verb. Plural subject = plural verb. ✅ The group of students is waiting. ❌ The group of students are waiting.
Its vs It's Its = possessive. It's = it is / it has. ✅ The dog wagged its tail. It's raining.
Your vs You're Your = possessive. You're = you are. ✅ Your book is here. You're welcome.
There/Their/They're There = place. Their = possession. They're = they are. ✅ There it is. Their house. They're coming.
To/Too/Two To = direction. Too = also/excess. Two = number. ✅ Go to the shop. Me too. Two apples.
Comma Splice Do not join two complete sentences with only a comma. ❌ It was raining, I stayed inside. ✅ It was raining, so I stayed inside.
Sentence Fragment Every sentence must have a subject and a verb. ❌ A large, empty room. ✅ She entered a large, empty room.
✍️ Writing Frameworks ▼

📝 PEEL Paragraph Structure

Step What to Do Example Sentence Starter
P — Point Make your point clearly "The writer uses a simile to describe the fog..."
E — Evidence Quote from the text "...'like a silent grey blanket'..."
E — Explain Explain the effect "...which suggests the fog covers everything quietly, creating a sense of unease."
L — Link Link back to the question or to the next point "This contributes to the overall atmosphere of suspense."

📝 Descriptive Writing — Senses Framework

Sense Questions to Ask Example
👁️ Sight What colours, shapes, movements can you see? "The sky was a bruised purple."
👂 Sound What can you hear? Loud, quiet, rhythmic? "The wind shrieked through the alley."
👃 Smell What odours are in the air? "The air smelt of salt and ozone."
✋ Touch What textures, temperatures, sensations? "The rain was cold and sharp on her skin."
👅 Taste What tastes are present? "She could taste the salt on her lips."

📖 Key Terminology

Essential exam terms from the reference tables above.

Simile
A comparison using 'like' or 'as'. Creates a vivid image by linking two different things.
Metaphor
A direct comparison stating one thing is another. Creates a powerful, often surprising image.
Personification
Giving human qualities to non-human objects or ideas. Makes the abstract feel alive.
Cyclical Structure
When the ending of a text mirrors its beginning. Creates a sense of completeness or inevitability.
Foreshadowing
Hints or clues about future events in the text. Builds tension and anticipation.
Pathetic Fallacy
When the weather or environment reflects the mood of a scene or character.
Juxtaposition
Placing two contrasting ideas, images, or characters close together for effect.
Tricolon
A list of three items or phrases. Creates rhythm, emphasis, and a sense of completeness.
Comma Splice
A grammatical error where two complete sentences are joined with only a comma instead of a conjunction or full stop.
Sentence Fragment
An incomplete sentence that lacks a subject, verb, or does not express a complete thought.
